#50: The Graveyard Book

January 1st, 2010 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Review | YA Fiction

TITLE: The Graveyard Book
AUTHOR: Neil Gaiman
PAGES: 320

It takes a village to raise a child, as they say, but for Nobody Owens…it took an entire graveyard. The night he was orphaned, his family was murdered and Bod was the only survivor. He was rescued by the spirits who inhabited the graveyard behind his home, and those same spirits endeavored to raise the child as their own. Bod grows up among a varied cast of characters, all of whom are dead, but none of whom are any less interesting because of it. He learns to melt into the shadows, how to walk through walls, and all about history– from the people who actually lived it. He learns about death, but in the end the biggest lesson is about life itself.

I love Neil Gaiman’s books, from Stardust to Coraline to the short stories posted on his website. (I also follow him on Twitter, he is really very funny.)Â The Graveyard Book was no exception, and I was thrilled for it to be my 50th book this year. Not only is the writing superb, as is characteristic of Neil Gaiman, but there are also wonderful illustrations scattered throughout the pages, and they fit the book and story perfectly. I think the biggest misconception you could make about Gaiman’s books is that they are targeted towards children, or the younger set in general. As an adult, I have always found his books to be satisfying, and while books like Coraline and The Graveyard Book are centered around children, there are plenty of undertones that remain eerie and fascinating even to someone who has left their childhood behind. I spent my time reading this book completely enamored of Bod’s unconventional graveyard family, especially the fact that all of these ghosts cherished and cared for Bod genuinely, and Bod loved them too. The Graveyard Book does a phenomenal job of looking further into the shadowy topics of death, dying, and what waits for us on the other side and making what would be morbid into something that is less frightening. But on the flipside of that coin, the book deals with life and how to live your life, as well. It is not a typical coming-of-age story, but I think that it totally deserves the Newbery Medal that it was awarded last year. Of course, it helps that the inspiration for the book was Rudyard Kipling’s classic The Jungle Book, and I loved that one as well. Overall, fantastic, amazing, highly recommended book– and author, as well.

RATING: 5/5
