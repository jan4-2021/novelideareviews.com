]]> http://novelideareviews.com/the-journey-home-week-2-discussion/feed/ 5 Not A Drop To Drink + Signed Bookmark Giveaway http://novelideareviews.com/not-a-drop-to-drink/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=not-a-drop-to-drink http://novelideareviews.com/not-a-drop-to-drink/#comments Thu, 17 Oct 2013 21:00:00 +0000 Paola http://novelideareviews.com/?p=3911 Read more →]]> TITLE: Not a Drop To Drink

AUTHOR: Mindy McGinnis

GENRE: YA Science Fiction/Dystopian

PUBLISHED: September 2013, Katherine Tegen Books

FORMAT: Hardcover, 320 Pages

RELATED POST: Dark Days Tour Recap

In a world where fresh water is rare enough to kill for, Lynn and her mother spend their days on the roof of their house. The focus of every day is defending the pond on their property, which is their main source of water, and which is consequently a prime target for others who are desperate to survive. Life is a battle for Lynn, and it only intensifies when shots in the dark and smoke on the horizon signal both a beginning and an end.

Admittedly, this book would’ve flown under my radar if not for my good friend Charlotte at Gypsy Reviews, who told me I needed to pick it up while I was at the Dark Days Tour signing last month! So thanks for the recommendation, Charlotte!! I was lucky enough to meet author Mindy McGinnis during the event — she signed my book (and tore the title page, but she drew a smiley face next to it so all was well in the end! haha) and also signed a bookmark for me to give away! Details on how to win are at the end of this post :) And be sure to read my recap of the Dark Days Tour stop in Vegas for what Mindy had to say about her debut novel!

Not a Drop To Drink is pretty stark on the surface — Lynn and her mother are fighting for survival in a bleak, dystopian society. I know a lot of us have had enough of dystopia in YA these days, but I have to say that this one stands out from the crowd. Not only is the survival aspect very realistic and believable, it’s also infused with an undercurrent of hope and the possibility of change for the better.

I liked that it was such a no-frills narrative. The writing is readable but not so simplistic that it detracts from the emotional content. You really get a feel for the kind of life Lynn leads, which is not forgiving or easy at all. The environment is harsh, riddled with dangers that need to be guarded against at all times. From a very young age, she’s been forced to sink or swim. Sometimes when I read dystopia, it’s really hard to get into the atmosphere because too many frivolous things litter the story — love triangles, insta-love, extraneous drama — all the usual suspects that make it seem like they’re too busy being emo to actually get to the business of surviving. This isn’t the case with Not a Drop To Drink. Every minute of every day is precious, and every day in itself is hard won.

The protagonist, Lynn, has never known a world that didn’t demand this kind of struggle. She’s been raised by her mother, learning to grow a thick skin and move through life without hesitating to kill if that’s what it takes to survive another day. They defend their pond against man and beast — it doesn’t matter who. And because she’s been raised to trust no one but her mother, Lynn is a character that sometimes displays a really callous personality. It kind of bothered me at times just how unfeeling she could be, but it’s worthwhile to remember that this is all she’s ever been taught.

Until events cause massive change in her life, Lynn is resistant to any type of change at all and even more resistant when it comes to depending on other people. She’s wary of going beyond the small circle of her comfort zone, and the crazy thing here is that what’s “comfortable” for Lynn would probably be SUPER uncomfortable for a big baby like me, haha. The good thing is that she undergoes this massive amount of growth by the time the story ends. She’s still tough as nails, and she still has a very black and white, straight up survivalist outlook on life, but her heart becomes more open. She experiences love and friendship that temper her previous experiences of loss and hardship.

There is a romance in this book, but it’s pretty low key. The highlight of the story is Lynn’s relationship with a little girl named Lucy who teaches her both how to care for another person and how to be cared for in return. I really appreciated this aspect of the story, as well as Lynn’s growing acceptance and eventual friendship with her neighbor, Stebbs. Overall, what makes this novel is not the stark reality of a world without fresh water and what that means; instead, it’s about fighting for the people you love.

RATING: 4/5

This is a solid standalone novel and one that I recommend for anyone looking for a true survival story — the author knows her stuff! As she mentioned during the Dark Days event, her actual lifestyle is very similar to Lynn’s! (Not because she shoots people from her roof if they come near her pond… haha!)

]]> http://novelideareviews.com/not-a-drop-to-drink/feed/ 4 A (Sort-Of) Blogging Manifesto http://novelideareviews.com/a-sort-of-blogging-manifesto/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=a-sort-of-blogging-manifesto http://novelideareviews.com/a-sort-of-blogging-manifesto/#comments Wed, 16 Oct 2013 07:00:00 +0000 Paola http://novelideareviews.com/?p=3957 Read more →]]> This is a post about the fine art of juggling. Specifically, the fine art of juggling my never-ending TBR pile, being a grown up, trying to write a manuscript, answering the question, “Teacher, is it Halloween yet?” 12 billion times a day, figuring out what to do for dinner, editing a gigantic pile of photos from my last session, AND finding time to blog, all at the same time. As you might guess, it’s a little bit ridiculous. Also, it’s exhausting.

September was probably the craziest month we’ve ever had on the blog to date, and you may have noticed a drop in the number of posts here around that last week or so. To put it simply, life attacked with a vengeance. I caught this new and particularly ferocious cold/virus/THING right around then (thanks, preschool) and it also happened to be when Alix ended up completely slammed at work. We were skipping scheduled posts left and right, drafts remaining unwritten and growing cold and lonely on our calendar while we struggled to maintain equilibrium in our hectic offline lives. We were silent on Twitter. We let the blog slip into a state of temporary hibernation.

It was really hard on me because upkeep of this blog is a pretty big deal in my life. Through this blog, I’ve made friends and discovered amazing books and made a lot of really incredible memories. Running it with Alix is my #1 hobby, out of all my dozens of hobbies; it’s kind of like this crazy unpaid job that I’ve devoted countless hours to, and I know that a lot of you out there feel the same way. I think that’s why, when the latest Goodreads debacle took place recently, I well and truly felt slapped in the face.

Honestly, I feel this way anytime book bloggers are accused of bullying, or being overly critical, or criticized themselves for (gasp!) having an opinion. I don’t think that’s fair, and here’s why: for so many of us, book blogging is a labor of love. We do this because we love books, because we have things to say about books, and because we want more than anything to share our love with the entire universe. At least, that’s what I’m in it for. I don’t do this for money. I don’t do this because I’m bored — far from it. I have a pretty full plate over here. But I don’t think I’m alone in saying that I blog because I love it, and I make time for it because of that. If it ever stops being fun, or if it ever starts to become more about what others think and want instead of what we think and want, I’ll be done blogging. Because that would render all of this pointless.

When book bloggers as a whole are put down for “bullying” and “author abuse” and other assorted crimes, I can’t help but wonder if all these people out there have any idea how much work we really put in — how much time and effort it takes, all the reading time we snag during lunch breaks and commutes because we committed to reviewing something, all the mental anguish when we love an author but not their book. The list goes on and on. I mean, it’s a complex equation. It doesn’t just amount to, “Oh, you’re mean because you didn’t like this book. You’re an awful, awful person and a bully as well.” I believe we have a right to express our feelings.

And really, what it amounts to is that we will always say and do whatever we like on our blog, regardless of what others might think is right or wrong when it comes to book blogging. It’s our time that we’ve invested, in reading and writing up posts and coming up with features. We could be doing other things, but we choose to do this. I know a lot of you out there feel the same way. So don’t lose sight of the reason why you blog, whatever that reason might be. Keep it with you even when life is crazy and you can’t stick to your schedule. Remember it when you feel discouraged by negativity and lack of support. None of that stuff matters as long as you’re still doing something that makes you happy. At least, that’s how I see things.

What about you? Why do you blog, and how do you juggle life + blogging? Do you think there’s a lack of appreciation for book bloggers in the industry?

TITLE: The Coldest Girl in Coldtown

AUTHOR: Holly Black

GENRE: YA Paranormal/Urban Fantasy

PUBLISHED: 2013; Little, Brown BFYR

SOURCE: Netgalley

Â Tana lives in a world where walled cities called Coldtowns exist. In them, quarantined monsters and humans mingle in a decadently bloody mix of predator and prey. The only problem is, once you pass through Coldtownâs gates, you can never leave.

One morning, after a perfectly ordinary party, Tana wakes up surrounded by corpses. The only other survivors of this massacre are her exasperatingly endearing ex-boyfriend, infected and on the edge, and a mysterious boy burdened with a terrible secret. Shaken and determined, Tana enters a race against the clock to save the three of them the only way she knows how: by going straight to the wicked, opulent heart of Coldtown itself.

It’s been such a long time since I read a vampire book I enjoyed this much — I was well and truly impressed with the gritty and yet oddly glamorous world of Holly Black’s vampires and the humans who co-exist with them. I loved that the story was full of both beauty and blood. I loved the entire pop culture phenomenon she created around vampirism. I loved her heroine. I JUST LOVED IT OKAY.

The thing about the world we see in this novel is that it’s so very believable, especially when you consider modern culture and the things society values and finds entertaining right now, as we speak. Holly Black’s vampires are relegated to Coldtowns, where they are walled away from the rest of the population but still the center of a universe that is inexorably drawn to their allure. Magnetic, fascinating, and infinitely dangerous, they are both feared and yet hopelessly admired. It was crazy seeing vampirism from this angle. There are vampire tourist traps, vampire blogs, vampire hunter shows littered with crossbows and imagery that ranges from the grotesquely fascinating to just plain grotesque.

Immortal, the vampires celebrate a night that never ends, which in turn is accessible to anyone with cable. This atmosphere was conveyed with masterful execution that gave the reader insight into a lot of different perspectives, from the blogger obsessed with being immortal to the rest of the population that lives outside all of that glory and gore, still striving to live the most normal lives possible in the wake of a vampirism epidemic.

This vividly rendered world would be pointless without the right kind of heroine, and I’m happy to say that Tana is absolutely right for her role. I loved that she was so determined to hang on to who she was, even when it would have been easier to give in and let go of every shred of humanity she possessed. She’s a fighter in so many ways. I liked her attitude, her practical acceptance that in order to get through this ordeal she would have to do things that would not only hurt her, but possibly hurt others as well. I guess I just like that kind of personality in my heroines. When you’re faced with a terrible situation, do you panic and fall apart, or do you get a grip and keep moving on? Tana chooses the latter. A lot of shit goes down, but she sticks to her ideals and what’s important to her. I can’t help but respect that in a character.

I mean, Tana manages to stay true to herself even with GAVRIEL to deal with. Guys, we can’t talk about him, okay? Because I will start screaming and flailing and just turn into a pile of slush. Here is a complex character in every sense of the word. We never know what’s going on with him. We’re never entirely sure where his allegiance lies. He proves to be both unpredictable and slightly deranged, but it’s somehow perfect. The attraction between these two is ridiculous and heart-stopping and again, somehow perfect. I have to say that Gavriel is one of my favorite leading men (and vampires) ever.

RATING: 4.5/5

Absolutely loved it. And it’s not part of a series, but this is one of those rare occasions when I kind of found myself wishing it was a series, because I just couldn’t get enough of the world and the characters (and Gavriel, ahem) in only one book. That being said, I also love that it was a standalone in a genre dominated by series. You get closure, even if you don’t get to have more delicious vampire men. /sigh/ Oh, fine. Anyway, highly recommended — definitely the best vampire book I’ve read since Robin McKinley’s Sunshine, which is saying something when you consider that Sunshine happens to be one of my favorite books of all time AND combines the immortal undead with tons of cinnamon rolls. I mean, could you get a higher recommendation from me based on this??? No. So go get this book!! (And Sunshine too, okay??)

Unlike my experience actually reading Finnikin of the Rock for the first time, the story behind how I discovered the book isn’t at all exciting. I devour fantasy books on a regular basis and I’m always looking for my next escape, which is what led me to Lumatere. I remember skimming through a few reviews on Goodreads and finding myself compelled by the sheer amount of people who were rendered incoherent and/or speechless by this book. This got me thinking that I should probably pick it up.

Some 400 pages later, I realized this had been one of the best literary moments of my life. EVER. But I’d packed my bags and applied for permanent citizenship long before that. Maybe it was the first time Evanjalin rolled her eyes at Finnikin. (And all the subsequent times hahaha) Maybe it was every single time these characters kept going, never giving up on the home they’d been forced to leave behind. I can’t quite put my finger on when it happened exactly, but somewhere on that journey I decided I belonged there too.

I think many of us who read fantasy are drawn to the genre because A) it gives us a chance to get away to an entirely different world and/or B) it gives us a chance to live in a world where the epic and the legendary are still possible. Lumatere exemplifies both, for me. This is a place where there is power in every promise, where honor and loyalty still matter, where love is unbreakable. These are people who do absolutely nothing by halves, whether it’s living or loving or dying. It’s where friendship is a bond that lasts forever, where I’ve witnessed every possible way to be brave.

It never ceases to amaze. Melina Marchetta is capable of rendering me into a sobbing, laughing, inarticulate mess with only one sentence. This universe of hers even seems to be rendered in colors more vivid than the ones in our spectrum, to the point that reading this series somehow made me feel more alive. And I guess this feeling just makes me want to take this book and give it to everyone I meet. I just want you to see what I see. I want you to experience this crazy, crushing, heartbreaking, exhilarating, BEAUTIFUL feeling. It’s why I came to Lumatere and never left.

If you missed the other two installments of these shenanigans, here’s Part I (introduction + chapters 1-7) and Part II (chapters 8-12).

PERSUASION: PART II

Chapters One & Two

Anne finally ends her forever long stay at Uppercross with the Musgroves and departs with her old chum (and supplier of the world’s worst advice) Lady Russell. But of course it’s a small, small world so she eventually sees the Musgroves again in Lyme. It is revealed that Benwick, Prince of Emo, has been asking about Anne. Also revealed: Anne’s crybaby hypochondriac sister Mary is still a massive jerk. Another new development is Cousin Elliott’s interest in suddenly reconciling with the rest of his family after alternating between ignoring and slandering them for the past few years. He’s been sighted in Bath, being a lurking lurker.

Chapter Three

Time for a new brand of misery as Anne reluctantly joins Sir Walter (King of the Douchebags) and Elizabeth (Queen of the Douchebags) at the Elliotts’ townhouse in Bath. Mrs. Clay, suspected gold digger, is still living at Camden Place with them. Bath is not Anne’s favorite place in the world, so of course the rest of her awful family absolutely loves it. Sir W and Elizabeth are obsessed with Cousin Elliott, conveniently forgiving all of his past sins now that he’s dedicated to being BFFs with them again. Anne has her misgivings about Cousin Elliott as well as Mrs. Clay, not that anyone is listening. She wonders if Cousin Elliott is interested in patching things up because he’s reconsidering marrying Elizabeth. Meanwhile, our wonderful Sir Walter spends some time lamenting the general ugliness of everyone else who lives in Bath.

In an interesting turn of events, Cousin Elliott pays a visit to Camden Place and realizes that Anne is, in fact, that cute girl he saw at the beach in Lyme not long ago. Anne is surprised to find that her first evening in Bath is less miserable than what she was expecting. Still miserable, but not AS miserable, anyway.

Chapter Four

Anne continues being the only one worried about Cousin Elliott’s intentions re: Elizabeth and Mrs. Clay’s gold digging schemes re: Sir Walter. Lady Russell seems to have shelved most of her worries, although she is still of the opinion that Mrs. Clay is a shameless social climber. The more superficial and pretentious members of the Elliott clan go into full freak out mode over the impending arrival of distant relations Lady Dalrymple and her daughter, who are nobility. Relations between the Elliotts and Dalrymples have been strained since the death of Lady Elliott ages ago, but in the end the fences are mended and Anne gets to listen to her father and sister fawning over them day and night. Cousin Elliott is not as disgusted by their behavior as Anne, sneakily pointing out that at least hanging out with the Dalrymples distracts Sir Walter from any possibility of interaction (or, grossly enough, falling in love) with Mrs. Clay.

Chapter Five

Now that she’s living in Bath, Anne decides to track down her former governess, Mrs. Smith. Poor Mrs. Smith is now both destitute and a cripple, but still a lovely person. Definitely better company than what Anne has to deal with at Camden Place. They have many a long talk after not seeing each other for so long. Anne admires Mrs. Smith’s ability to remain hopeful despite the tidal wave of horrible luck that destroyed her life in recent years. Of course, because Anne likes Mrs. Smith, her father and sister do not. Who didn’t see that coming?? We are treated to a rank from Sir W about his hatred of low-ranked widows and Mrs. Clay leaves the room before he can realize that he is in the presence of a low-ranked widow. /awkward

Cousin Elliott continues to express that he thinks Anne is awesome. Anne is weirded out because honestly, when does anyone ever say she’s awesome?? Or even acknowledges her presence at all?? Lady Russell believes that Cousin E may have romantic intentions towards Anne, perhaps with plans to pursue her rather than Elizabeth. She is thrilled. Anne is not. She tells Lady R as much, but leaves out the part where she obviously still suffers from Wentworth Fever like all the rest of us. (WENTWORTH I MISS YOU PLEASE COME BACK INTO THE STORY SOON)

Chapter Six

This chapter opens with a nice, whiny letter from Mary — oh joy. That’s what you wanted to start off with, right? Appended to Mary’s usual Tales of Hypochondria and general lamentations as to how everyone else has a more awesome life than she does, we are given the news that Louisa is finally recovered and back at Uppercross… AND BENWICK IS IN LOVE WITH HER! Mary takes some time to gloat about how she was totally right that Benwick wasn’t ever interested in Anne at all; I take some time to stare at the page and then write “WHAT!!! GAMECHANGER!!!” in my notes. Aside from Mary being her usual tacky self, Anne is very pleased with the letter. She is happy for Benwick and Louisa. In place of our scheduled rant from Sir Walter, we get to hear Elizabeth say some rather uncharitable things about sailors.

While on a walk through the charming streets of Bath, Anne encounters none other than Admiral Croft, now the family’s tenant at Kellynch Hall and one of her favorite people. Admiral Croft is deep in a conversation with himself in which he criticizes a painting of a ship in a shop window, which he thinks is all wrong. When he realizes that Anne is there, he invites her for a walk and they discuss the crazy news that Louisa and Benwick are now engaged.
