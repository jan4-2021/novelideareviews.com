Chapter Seven

Oh snap! Mrs. Clay, are you now gunning for Cousin Elliott instead of Sir Walter??! You gold-digging minx, you! Not that this worked since Cousin E decided to go walking in the rain with Anne. Which, of course, is when Wentworth finally decides to come back into our story! (A wild Wentworth appears! SNORT) After seeing this other dude taking walks in the rain with Anne, Wentworth decides that he ALSO wants to do this. Plus he wants to show off his shiny new umbrella. But Cousin E interrupts the conversation, and a stroll with Anne is not in the cards for Wentworth.

As the days pass, Wentworth sightings continue to take place all around Bath. Even Lady Russell takes some time to stare at him. She covers this up by pretending to be staring at some really snazzy drawing room curtains instead. Smooth, Lady R. Anne says nothing about this but does make some private commentary on “the elegant stupidity of private parties.” Because she’s Anne, and sometimes snarky, even if she doesn’t ever say these things out loud.

By the end of the chapter, everyone is in full freak-out mode over an upcoming concert. Anne doesn’t really want to go… until she realizes that Wentworth might be there. #Gamechanger

Chapter Eight

Â Wentworth does indeed attend the concert. He and Anne have a brief chat about Benwick and Louisa’s surprise engagement, during which Wentworth expresses his own concerns that the two aren’t alike enough. He can’t believe how easily Benwick got over his fiancee, the tragic Fanny Harville; “A man does not recover from such devotion of the heart to such a woman!” OH REALLY???? HMMMMMMMMMM

Anyway, Wentworth adds that he does feel responsible for what happened to Louisa. Anne makes some sympathetic noises but mostly just shrugs and is like, “Ah, well, but I do hope I get to go back to Lyme someday cause that place was awesome. Just saying.” This is because she’s secretly rendered incoherent by Wentworth’s mini-speech about never being able to get over certain women one falls in love with. Is there hope in this awkward, angsty situation after all??!!! Except before any more of this riveting conversation can continue, Cousin Elliott appears on the scene and proceeds to go through with THE MOST UNWANTED INTERRUPTION OF ALL TIME. SERIOUSLY, DUDE?????

A dismayed Anne watches Wentworth return to his own seat while Cousin Elliott starts confessing that he’s heard all sorts of lovely things about Anne and that he thinks very highly of her AND ALSO BTW I’M GOING TO SORT OF PROPOSE TO YOU NOW IN THE MIDDLE OF THIS CONCERT. (Yes, this happened.) Anne rendered speechless; resolves to flee ASAP. After intermission, she makes up her mind to go sit next to Wentworth and all the readers in the world stand up and just cheer for hours on end UNTIL COUSIN ELLIOTT COMES BACK TO RUIN THE MOMENT AGAIN DAMNIT GO AWAY JSKDHFKJSDHF

SDHFFHJSGDFHJSFKJHDJKFH

Chapter Nine

In the days following the concert, Anne devotes all her energy to avoiding Cousin Elliott like the plague. She decides to visit her old friend Mrs. Smith, her former governess, who (if you remember from last installment) is now a destitute cripple but still a very sweet lady. She figures no one in her family, including Cousin E, would be caught dead consorting with destitute, crippled widows and that Mrs. Smith’s house is the safest place to hide right now. But when she gets there, Mrs. Smith starts being super weird about the whole Cousin E thing, going so far as to tell Anne that she expected to hear news of an engagement that day, and that she thinks Cousin E is a great guy, et al etc. Anne is confused and chooses to investigate further, whereupon Mrs. Smith suddenly pulls a complete 180 and condemns Cousin E as a Grade A Douchebag… what is going on??!!

After a considerable amount of hemming and hawing, Mrs Smith divulges that she has known Cousin E for quite some time. In fact, he used to be a very close friend of hers through her late husband, back when Cousin E was much less monetarily endowed and would come hang out with them/mooch off their food and hospitality all the time. Apparently he married his first wife (now also deceased) for her money rather than marrying Anne’s sister Elizabeth way back when. Mrs Smith produces an incriminating letter that proves what a lying, scheming, gold digging brigand Cousin Elliott really is. Behold, a list of his dastardly crimes:

  • Assisted in ruining the Smith family finances, eventually leading to the demise of Mr. Smith and leaving Mrs. Smith a destitute, crippled widow.
  • Was the executor of Mr. Smith’s will, and yet refuses to assist Mrs. Smith in claiming property and money that is due to her and which might just reverse her fortunes.
  • Decided to re-insinuate himself in the Elliott family’s good graces when he realized that Mrs. Clay, that (fellow) conniving, gold-digging minx, had plans to try and marry Sir Walter. And lady, this town ain’t big enough for the both of us. /cue gunfight in Austen novel
  • Is now planning to foil Mrs. Clay’s marriage prospects AND marry Anne himself…!

After listening to all this, Anne realizes just how close she came to being persuaded YET AGAIN to make the wrong decision. If she’d listened to Lady Russell’s babbling about how awesome it would be to marry Cousin Elliott and become mistress of Kellynch Hall just like her mother used to be, Anne would be in the same capsizing ship all over again. She resolves to tell Lady R everything she’s just learned about the true depths of Cousin E’s utter douchebaggery.

Chapter Ten

Like a true professional Queen Douchebag, Elizabeth manages to “send her love” to Lady Russell while simultaneously putting down her taste in books and ridiculing her fashion sense. Hey, this kind of jackass behavior takes skill, okay? Speaking of jackasses, Sir Walter tacks on HIS love to Lady Russell while also criticizing how disheveled she looks in the morning. Ah, the apple doesn’t fall far from the tree~

With perfect timing, another bad apple rolls into town — none other than our favorite crybaby hypochondriac, Mary! Right behind her are a bunch of the Musgroves visiting from Uppercross. Anne’s visit to Lady R is put off as she is pulled into social calls with the Musgroves and learns all about the upcoming weddings planned for Louisa and Henrietta. She is genuinely happy for both of the girls and mostly enjoys seeing everyone again (minus Mary, cause who would really want to see her??) but of course Wentworth soon arrives to have dinner with them all and brings a whole lot of Awkward with him. Anne and Wentworth are careful to stand on opposite sides of the room while secretly wrestling with inner turmoil. Mary, renowned busybody, happens to glance out the window and see some sort of furtive exchange going on between Mrs Clay and Cousin Elliott out on the street. This gets everyone talking about Anne and Cousin E; Anne is consequently upset that all of Bath seems to think she’s marrying that punk. Eeewwwwww.

After the pleasure of watching Mary and her husband have another of their fun little marital squabbles, Sir William and Elizabeth crash the party in order to invite everyone to THEIR party on the following night. Because, appropriate.

Chapter Eleven

On the following day, Anne is hanging out at the hotel with the visitors from Uppercross. Mrs. Musgrove and Mrs. Croft (wife of Admiral Croft, tenant at the Elliott family’s estate, Kellynch Hall) are avidly discussing short engagements versus long engagements and seem to favor the latter option. Long engagements are apparently the worst idea ever. Wentworth pretends he isn’t listening. Pffftttt WHATEVER, WENTWORTH. Captain Harville (seafaring friend of Wentworth) randomly starts talking to Anne about his late sister Fanny, who was engaged to Benwick. He shows Anne a portrait of Benwick that had been commissioned for Fanny, but which is now being made over for Benwick’s new fiancee Louisa Musgrove. He laments that Fanny would not have “forgotten” Benwick as quickly as Benwick forgot about her.

Anne agrees, and in her BEST speech ever in the whole book, she makes a fantastic argument basically stating that women do not forget love as easily as men do. Wentworth continues to pretend that he isn’t listening…

BUT THEN HE PASSES HER A NOTE (MIDDLE SCHOOL STYLE) AND IT IS THE BEST NOTE EVER AND JUST EEEEEKKKKKKKKKKKK KJDHFKSJDHFKJSDHFKJSDFHKJSDHF THIS IS THE SOUND OF ME DYING OF THE FEELS

(in my notes this moment is marked by me writing the word EEEEEKKKKKKK in gigantic all-caps letters that take up like 5 lines)

Chapter Twelve

WENTWORTH AND ANNE RENEW THEIR ENGAGEMENT. YESSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSS

Sir Walter finally writes this down in his precious book of the British Peerage (LOL). Lady Russell is made to writhe in agony as her long-ago prediction of doom and misfortune is proven COMPLETELY WRONG. Ha!!! Mary, of course, decides to take all the credit for her sister’s match with Wentworth but also suffers quite a bit of jealousy over Anne’s shiny new carriage. Cousin Elliott leaves Bath; and, since gold-digging birds of a feather flock together, it seems that Mrs. Clay has switched from chasing Sir Walter to chasing Cousin Elliott. Eeewwww. Despite feeling bad that she’s causing Wentworth to marry into a rather shitty family, Anne lives happily ever after with her beloved Wentworth (and a lifetime case of Wentworth Fever ;D). YAY HAPPY ENDING!!!

Hello again, and welcome to Part III of Paola’s translation of Persuasion by Jane Austen (for the Austen-Challenged!) as part of our participation in Classics Retold! If you missed the other two installments of these shenanigans, here’s Part I (introduction + chapters 1-7) and Part II (chapters 8-12).

PERSUASION: PART II

Chapters One & Two

Anne finally ends her forever long stay at Uppercross with the Musgroves and departs with her old chum (and supplier of the world’s worst advice) Lady Russell. But of course it’s a small, small world so she eventually sees the Musgroves again in Lyme. It is revealed that Benwick, Prince of Emo, has been asking about Anne. Also revealed: Anne’s crybaby hypochondriac sister Mary is still a massive jerk. Another new development is Cousin Elliott’s interest in suddenly reconciling with the rest of his family after alternating between ignoring and slandering them for the past few years. He’s been sighted in Bath, being a lurking lurker.

Chapter Three

Time for a new brand of misery as Anne reluctantly joins Sir Walter (King of the Douchebags) and Elizabeth (Queen of the Douchebags) at the Elliotts’ townhouse in Bath. Mrs. Clay, suspected gold digger, is still living at Camden Place with them. Bath is not Anne’s favorite place in the world, so of course the rest of her awful family absolutely loves it. Sir W and Elizabeth are obsessed with Cousin Elliott, conveniently forgiving all of his past sins now that he’s dedicated to being BFFs with them again. Anne has her misgivings about Cousin Elliott as well as Mrs. Clay, not that anyone is listening. She wonders if Cousin Elliott is interested in patching things up because he’s reconsidering marrying Elizabeth. Meanwhile, our wonderful Sir Walter spends some time lamenting the general ugliness of everyone else who lives in Bath.

In an interesting turn of events, Cousin Elliott pays a visit to Camden Place and realizes that Anne is, in fact, that cute girl he saw at the beach in Lyme not long ago. Anne is surprised to find that her first evening in Bath is less miserable than what she was expecting. Still miserable, but not AS miserable, anyway.

Chapter Four

Anne continues being the only one worried about Cousin Elliott’s intentions re: Elizabeth and Mrs. Clay’s gold digging schemes re: Sir Walter. Lady Russell seems to have shelved most of her worries, although she is still of the opinion that Mrs. Clay is a shameless social climber. The more superficial and pretentious members of the Elliott clan go into full freak out mode over the impending arrival of distant relations Lady Dalrymple and her daughter, who are nobility. Relations between the Elliotts and Dalrymples have been strained since the death of Lady Elliott ages ago, but in the end the fences are mended and Anne gets to listen to her father and sister fawning over them day and night. Cousin Elliott is not as disgusted by their behavior as Anne, sneakily pointing out that at least hanging out with the Dalrymples distracts Sir Walter from any possibility of interaction (or, grossly enough, falling in love) with Mrs. Clay.

Chapter Five

Now that she’s living in Bath, Anne decides to track down her former governess, Mrs. Smith. Poor Mrs. Smith is now both destitute and a cripple, but still a lovely person. Definitely better company than what Anne has to deal with at Camden Place. They have many a long talk after not seeing each other for so long. Anne admires Mrs. Smith’s ability to remain hopeful despite the tidal wave of horrible luck that destroyed her life in recent years. Of course, because Anne likes Mrs. Smith, her father and sister do not. Who didn’t see that coming?? We are treated to a rank from Sir W about his hatred of low-ranked widows and Mrs. Clay leaves the room before he can realize that he is in the presence of a low-ranked widow. /awkward

Cousin Elliott continues to express that he thinks Anne is awesome. Anne is weirded out because honestly, when does anyone ever say she’s awesome?? Or even acknowledges her presence at all?? Lady Russell believes that Cousin E may have romantic intentions towards Anne, perhaps with plans to pursue her rather than Elizabeth. She is thrilled. Anne is not. She tells Lady R as much, but leaves out the part where she obviously still suffers from Wentworth Fever like all the rest of us. (WENTWORTH I MISS YOU PLEASE COME BACK INTO THE STORY SOON)

Chapter Six

This chapter opens with a nice, whiny letter from Mary — oh joy. That’s what you wanted to start off with, right? Appended to Mary’s usual Tales of Hypochondria and general lamentations as to how everyone else has a more awesome life than she does, we are given the news that Louisa is finally recovered and back at Uppercross… AND BENWICK IS IN LOVE WITH HER! Mary takes some time to gloat about how she was totally right that Benwick wasn’t ever interested in Anne at all; I take some time to stare at the page and then write “WHAT!!! GAMECHANGER!!!” in my notes. Aside from Mary being her usual tacky self, Anne is very pleased with the letter. She is happy for Benwick and Louisa. In place of our scheduled rant from Sir Walter, we get to hear Elizabeth say some rather uncharitable things about sailors.

While on a walk through the charming streets of Bath, Anne encounters none other than Admiral Croft, now the family’s tenant at Kellynch Hall and one of her favorite people. Admiral Croft is deep in a conversation with himself in which he criticizes a painting of a ship in a shop window, which he thinks is all wrong. When he realizes that Anne is there, he invites her for a walk and they discuss the crazy news that Louisa and Benwick are now engaged. Amusingly enough, Anne is the only person in the world who thinks these two are meant to be with each other; everyone else is like WHAT!!!! GAMECHANGER!!! The topic of Louisa naturally ends up leading Admiral Croft to the related topic of Wentworth. He suggests that they invite Wentworth to visit them in Bath…

Don’t get me wrong— I think that if I had read this book when I was a bit younger, I would have gotten right on board with the Scarlett fan club. It isn’t that I don’t love her, because I do, but I am more of a Melanie fan. You would have to read the book to find out more about Melanie, but suffice it to say that she was half dead with illness and yet still stood at the top of the stairs with her dead brother’s sword in her hand, ready to kill whoever was trying to rob their house. Not that she had to raise that sword, due to Scarlett conveniently shooting the robber in the face, but this is precisely where my conflict lies. I admire Scarlett’s drive, her ruthlessness, because there is a part of me that would probably be the exact same way given the situation. At the same time, I admire Melanie’s will of steel simply because she outwardly does not show how strong she really is. She’s strong in a different way.

But anyway, that paragraph is just one part of why I really loved this book. It is written with so much skill, there is so much power in the words and so much depth to every character. There is no relationship that can be taken at face value, not even relationships that seem easy to figure out at first glance. Just as I love Scarlett for the fire in her eyes, I fear her for the burning determination to take what she wants regardless of what she must destroy to achieve it. Just as I feel the heartbreak of the South at the death of everything it holds dear, I can’t forgive it for the slavery that built its mansions. The book is complicated. So is life. So was the Civil War. There is no better piece of literature to convey this. It was so good, I went on a total Civil War stretch this year hahahahaha. (You’ll see in later book reviews…)

In any case, someday when you feel ready to handle the beast that is Gone with the Wind, DO IT. And when you have finished it, run and pick up the two official sequels/companion novels, as authorized by the estate of Margaret Mitchell: Scarlett and Rhett Butler’s People.

Oh. All this time I didn’t even mention Rhett Butler and my undying love for him. WELL. Make that Selling Point #457. Hahahaha.

RATING: 5/5

AUTHOR: Muriel Spark

PAGES: 160

Unorthodox at best, Jean Brodie is the most controversial teacher at the rather conservative Marcia Blaine School for Girls. Her eccentricity and strong beliefs, combined with her insistence that she is currently in the “prime” of her life, lead her to assemble a close group of her favorite students in an attempt to pass on her knowledge and expertise. Seeking to mold these young women into her ideals, she brings out what she sees as the best in them while also looking at each of them as a personal triumph for herself. Passionate in everything from her attractions to married men, her affairs with other instructors, her blatant disregard for society’s rules and norms, and above all in living at her “prime,” Jean Brodie inspires each of the girls to a nearly cult-like devotion. They would follow her to victory or ruin— but one of them will choose to betray her.

I read this book astonishingly slowly, considering it wasn’t very long. There are some extremely good quotes from it that I ended up not being able to write down thanks to my highly abbreviated reading window at the time, which was approximately 30 minutes while I bolted down lunch and waited to pick up my first graders from the playground. In any case, the story of Jean Brodie’s rise and fall at the hands of her own beloved students is a balance of tedious and intriguing. I found it interesting how she found a spark of something special in each of her favorites, but even then these things about them were not what defined them by the end. It was a testament to the fact that anything can happen in life. Jean Brodie could tell the girls what was special about them, could put them on the path to greatness, but she could not control their feet as they tread upon it. Nor could she have guessed that one of her chosen few would bring her to ruin. In all, an okay book. This is usually how I feel about books that are supposedly classics, so I wasn’t all that surprised. The funny thing is, I actually agreed with a lot of Miss Brodie’s teaching methods, which were definitely more open-minded than was appropriate in the setting of the story. But, there is a reason why they tell you not to pick favorites as a teacher…haha.

RATING: 2.5/5

Has there ever been another woman half as enchanting as Holly Golightly? She drinks her milk in a wine goblet, calls everyone darling, climbs through windows, hides behind a brilliant smile and giant sunglasses, slips random French phrases into exclamations about cats, and wears elbow length gloves as she eats breakfast in front of the display window of Tiffany’s in New York City. Captivating many men while belonging to virtually none of them, Holly goes about life with an attitude all her own. Immortalized by the legendary Audrey Hepburn in the film of the same name, there is more to this exuberant and timeless character than her outward nonchalance and the impeccable wardrobe she dons every morning. Holly wants nothing more than a place that feels like home, a place that makes her feel as grand as Tiffany’s. But to what ends will she go, to achieve this dream? And what has she been willing to become?

One of the best decisions I’ve ever made was buying this DVD and watching it. At the time, I had no clue it was actually a novella before it was adapted for film, and so I didn’t think to look for a book until this year. The book is very much the same as the movie, and I feel like if I hadn’t already seen Audrey Hepburn playing Holly Golightly on screen, I would still not be able to envision anyone else in that role. I think, after reading and being able to compare, that Truman Capote’s Holly is a much darker character than the Holly portrayed by Hepburn; in the book, Holly is tangled in the same crazy problems, wrestling with the same identity crisis, but somehow I felt sadness and vulnerability in Audrey Hepburn’s version of Holly as opposed to the raw despair and sharp ruthlessness that radiated from Holly in the book. Not that it matters, because in the end Holly Golightly is still one of my absolute favorite people ever, especially since she’s the source of some of my absolute favorite quotes ever, such as the universally appropriate, “Golly gee damn!” If you have not already read the book or seen the film, what are you waiting for?? Highly recommended! The book itself is technically only a novella, which makes it a brief read, but enjoyable nonetheless.

RATING: 4/5
