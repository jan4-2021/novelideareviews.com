AUTHOR: Jay Kristoff

GENRE: Fantasy, Steampunk

SOURCE: ARC from Publisher, 437 pages

PRECEDED BY: Stormdancer

The fate of Shima hangs in the balance, with the Imperium on the brink of civil war and people of all walks of life rising to answer the Stormdancer’s call. The sequels this year are KILLING ME. SERIOUSLY. I mean, I’m sitting here right now staring at the screen, trying to figure out how to articulate the intense experience that was Kinslayer and just utterly failing. It’s hard to write a review when you’re sort of still peeling yourself off the floor and trying to salvage what’s left of your crushed heart. I thought Stormdancer was epic, heartbreaking, GLORIOUS — but its sequel somehow found a way to be all those things and more.

This book hits the ground running, and the action doesn’t stop until the very end. It’s a breathless, heart-stopping ride. When I read the first book earlier this year, I remember feeling initially overwhelmed by the author’s attention to detail; it was a lot to take in and process within those first hundred or so pages, and the one aspect of Stormdancer that bothered me. But with Kinslayer, that attention to detail becomes an incredible asset. This is an intense sensory experience that just consumes you. Everything, from the black depths of Yukiko’s grief to Shima’s bleak, broken landscape is rendered in such a powerful way that the colors seem twice as vivid and the emotions just leave you reeling. For the record, it’s apparently an alternate version of our world, with an alternate history in which everyone is born with two souls. One soul is dominant, the other recessive, and people who don’t end up losing their recessive soul are considered to be hybrids — extremely dangerous, and definitely not welcome in Addie and Eva’s alternate America. Here’s the thing, though: I have no idea why hybrids are supposedly so dangerous. They never seemed to have any unorthodox abilities, no telekinetic powers or superhuman strength, et al etc. In fact, the only clear advantage I saw in being hybrid was having double the brain power to get through algebra homework faster. (No joke, Addie and Eva actually provide an example of this in the book.)

So, case in point, if hybrids can’t really DO anything differently than non-hybrids… why on earth do we care if they live in our country or not?? Why exactly are they a threat?? I mean, did I miss something…? It was made rather obvious that foreigners were NOT wanted because foreign hybrids started a terrible war that nearly wiped out all of humanity. Fine, hybrids made bad choices about nuclear warfare. But the war is over, and yet the government seems to be harboring this borderline childish grudge against hybrids. I’m sure there’s an intended message here about how deeply society can fear and reject what they don’t understand, but I didn’t feel like it was executed well at all.

Wait, back to the world building. Which is barely there at all. Like I said, I had no idea at first whether this was a post-apocalyptic version of OUR world or a world of the author’s fabrication. I had to go back to the synopsis and see that it was indeed an alternate. I personally disliked that I was being forced to grasp at straws here. I wish there had been more clues that this wasn’t a world based on ours, because I think I would have been less confused and more able to get into the setting right from the start. Of course, I may be the only one upset about this aspect. World building is pretty important to me though, and it’s hard to portray a dystopia in an alternate world when you only allow your reader a very shallow immersion in that world. It detracts from the atmosphere.

I liked Eva’s will to survive, and I applauded her conviction to stay with Addie rather than eventually vanishing the way recessive souls are supposed to. She had these flashes of brilliance during which she actually did things; Addie, on the other hand, seemed more recessive in nature than Eva. I’m not sure what the rules are for deciding which soul is dominant vs recessive, as yet again the world building kind of failed to elaborate on this, but I really questioned how Addie was dominant when she spent more time twiddling her thumbs, hesitating about what to do next, than Eva ever did. My only strong impressions of Addie were during the times she lashed out at Eva for A) wanting to be more than a voice in the back of her head (gasp, how atrocious) and B) wanting to kiss a boy that Addie wasn’t interested in. ??? ?? ????

RATING: 2.5/5

Guys, I get the massive conflict that exists when you have two souls trapped in one body. That is actually a really intriguing idea to ponder. I get that it’s terrible never having private thoughts, never just getting to be you, because you have two personalities. In the end, it’s hard for either of them to be happy. I just wish this internal conflict had been paired with a plot that was more compelling and a setting that was more defined. Don’t get me wrong — I’m all for thought-provoking books and having to dig a little deeper to find the meaning of a story, but despite trying really hard to get something out of this experience, I’m sad to say I walked away pretty disappointed. Another case of having an awesome premise but falling short in the execution, in my opinion.
