What We Saw At Night

Published by Paola on March 3, 2013 | Leave a response

TITLE: What We Saw At Night

AUTHOR: Jacquelyn Mitchard

FORMAT: eBook, 218 pages

The world is different at night. Allie and her two closest friends, Rob and Juliet, know this better than anyone. The three all share the same genetic condition: Xeroderma Pigmentosum, a fatal allergy to sunlight. The only world they can truly live in is the one that emerges when the sun goes down. They have been friends forever, banding together against the disease that has every possibility of claiming their lives before they can even begin to truly live. But being unable to tolerate daylight doesn’t prevent the trio from trying to get as much out of life as they possibly can. They explore the streets and unearth buried secrets by the light of the moon, having the run of Iron Harbor from dusk until the last few hours before dawn. But it’s when they take up parkour, a stunt sport involving climbing and vaulting off tall buildings, that the thrill factor hits the ceiling. And it’s because of this need for a thrill that they find themselves witnessing something they shouldn’t have seen, what no Daytimer would ever have known about: murder.

The idea behind this book is, I think, brilliant. It covers rarely explored territory, promises a suspenseful plot. This is why I was drawn to it in the first place. And it isn’t that the plot lacks action or mystery, because it has those things — it just all suffers from poor execution. What We Saw At Night falls victim to a lot of tension that ultimately fizzes out far too soon.

#9: Coraline

Published by Paola on February 12, 2009 | Leave a response

TITLE: Coraline
AUTHOR: Neil Gaiman
PAGES: 162

The Pink Palace is Coraline’s new home, and because Coraline is an explorer, she has already discovered quite a few things about it. There is an old well that is supposedly a mile or two deep; a fairy ring surrounded by toadstools that smell awful if you step on one; an old man who tells her he is training his mice to perform their own circus; two elderly ladies who were once big stars on the stage (but now stay home reading tea leaves and doting on their three pet dogs)…and a mysterious door that leads to a brick wall. For an explorer, these are all amazing finds, particularly the door, but no one will listen to Coraline. Her mother and father are inattentive and make her eat mysterious gourmet food for dinner. So when Coraline finds herself dreaming of walking through the door and being greeted by someone who calls herself the Other Mother, the little girl can’t believe her luck. Because the Other Mother and Other Father seem to love her so much more than her true parents do…but what they ask of her in return may be far more costly than she could ever imagine.

My first question is, how is this a children’s book??? If I had read this as a child, I would have been scared to death and sleeping with all my lights on for weeks…or maybe I’m just exceptionally cowardly, hahaha. In any case, Coraline is bracketed for the little ones, but the tale itself is compelling enough for the older set. I wanted to read this before we went to see the recently released movie (which, btw, was awesome in 3D!), and now that I’ve seen both, I really have to say that the book was far more eerie; the movie was clearly adjusted for an audience of children. In the novel, Coraline’s fight to free herself, her parents, and the souls of the Other Mother’s past victims is much more terrifying. There are layers of meaning within the strange world’s illusions and shadowy monsters, shadows that are a nameless fear for a child, but contain different dimensions for adults. The story has more weight, is more serious, with consequences that aren’t slightly masked as they are in the movie version. The Other Mother’s previous conquests did not merely disappear or fall asleep—they died, and they will never come back. Like I said, these are heavy concepts for a child to understand, so I’m not entirely sure I would have a child read this…haha. Coraline is a dark fairytale that will have you wondering just what lies behind the doors in your own sleeping house…

RATING: 5/5

#26: SUNSHINE

Published by Paola on November 2, 2008 | Leave a response

TITLE: Sunshine
AUTHOR: Robin McKinley

In the world of Sunshine, devastating wars have been fought between Humans and what are called Others—denizens of supernatural, paranormal, and otherwise magical heritage. Vampires control most of the world’s wealth, lurking in the shadows and managing their assets in the dark of night. Rae, known affectionately around her stepfather’s coffeehouse as Sunshine, contentedly rises bright and early every morning to bake cinnamon rolls. Her life as an enterprising baker is turned abruptly upside down, however, when a late drive to the lake results in an encounter with vampires, getting chained to the wall of a deserted mansion while clad only in a blood red silk gown, and meeting yet another vampire: the mysterious Constantine. In the aftermath of Sunshine’s two days as a vampire’s sole companion, her fate takes on a number of turns she never expected…

This, my friends, is the novel Stephenie Meyer tried so desperately to write, and never achieved. I could leave my review at that, honestly. That one sentence explains the entire experience. I love Twilight, if not the rest of the books in that series, but Robin McKinley makes Stephenie Meyer look like a total amateur. McKinley’s post-apocalyptic world is full of both intense darkness and blinding light; the conflicts she presents between what is natural and what is not, between what it means to be human and what it is to be a vampire, are fleshed out and made real in the characters themselves. Once again, the world she creates is alive and breathing (or not breathing, if you want to count the undead). It leaps off the page and sends you reeling in. Constantine, while not being described as having any kind of attractive qualities, nevertheless becomes EXCEEDINGLY ATTRACTIVE, through his words and his actions alone.

Beauty is not the focal point for attraction in this novel. And while I’m not saying there was no substance in the attraction between Edward and Bella (maybe for Edward there was, at the very least…who the heck knows about Bella…), you can’t deny that the attraction between Sunshine and Constantine is so full of substance and depth and SOMETHING MORE. Their relationship is a friendship, a partnership, a pact. It’s a promise given and written in heart’s blood. And while the romantic aspect of their relationship isn’t exactly spelled out in so many words, nor is it ever really confirmed…you can’t help but feel it, like an undercurrent, in every interaction between them.

That takes skill, my friend. And Robin McKinley has it. Good grief, now I know she could write about anything and I’d probably devour it in one sitting. Absolutely PERFECT.

RATING: 5/5

#25: DIARY

Published by Paola on November 2, 2008 | Leave a response

TITLE: Diary
AUTHOR: Chuck Palahniuk

Misty Kleinman’s one dream in life was a vision: a beautiful island, with gorgeous houses and pristine beaches, where tourists sunned themselves and the streets fell in neat rows. Growing up in a trailer with a mother who worked multiple jobs for a living, Misty focused her artistic talent on endless drawings and paintings and sketches of this dream island, never imagining that she would eventually end up in art school, meet Peter Wilmot, and marry him on the same island she had always escaped to in her daydreams. But those daydreams turned swiftly to nightmares when Peter attempted suicide, saddling Misty with care of their daughter and Peter’s elderly mother, as well as a growing list of complaints and lawsuits from Peter’s clients…who are discovering whole rooms in their houses to be missing, and obscene messages on the walls once the rooms are uncovered. Misty makes no secret of how pissed she is about this in the “coma diary” her mother-in-law pushes her to write. It is through the diary that we discover the story behind Misty and Peter’s marriage, and unfolding clues regarding the sinister mysteries of the island itself.

Okay, loved this book. It was creepy, true. But it was exceedingly well written and the plot kept me involved to the very end. I always considered Chuck Palahniuk books to be more masculine fare than I would like, even though I really liked Fight Club. This book, however, was a pleasant surprise as well. It has just the right amount of creepiness and suspense and psychological issues. Parts of it are just plain eerie. I haven’t often run into books this well-crafted, and I have to say that I’ll probably pick up a few more Palahniuk books in the future. In all, an excellent novel and a very good scare at times.

RATING: 4/5
