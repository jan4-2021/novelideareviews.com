Chime

February 25th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | YA Fiction

TITLE: Chime

AUTHOR: Franny Billingsley

FORMAT: eBook, 298 pages

I’m being an idiot and trying to write this review too soon after finishing the book. I’m always really inarticulate and incoherent right after reading something I loved, so bear with me. I will try to extol to you the many virtues of Chime and its odd, clever, funny, headstrong, heroine. Or anti-heroine. I’m sure she would have a stronger inclination towards the latter.

Stepmother warned Briony never to tell father what happened. He wouldn’t understand, she said, and of course that was true. If father knew how wicked Briony was, how truly depraved, things would not go well for her. And it wouldn’t just be father who would turn on her. In the Swampsea, right on the edge of a bog filled with all manner of eerie fey creatures, no one would take kindly to the truth that Briony is a witch. Witches are hanged in the square for all to see. Thus, Briony keeps silent, and she stays away from the swamp. Stepmother warned her about that too. But there’s no way for stepmother to protect her now, because she is dead. Briony goes on living her life, which seems to darken daily as she contemplates all the things she must atone for. There’s her sister Rose, known in their village for being “not quite right in the head.” There’s the father she never speaks to if she can help it. And of course there are the memories: twisted, fragmented memories of what really happened the night Stepmother died. The truth is buried deep, but the arrival of a boy named Eldric and a threat that encroaches on the person Briony has sworn to protect might just be enough to bring it all to light.

Before you decide to read this book, bear in mind that it’s written in somewhat unconventional narration. Other readers have described it as odd, and it definitely is. The story is told in Briony’s voice, and it’s the voice of a girl who’s been trapped in her own head for far too long. She’s filled with bitterness and guilt, with self-loathing and remorse. After all, she believes herself to be the cause of her family’s suffering, past and present. She carries all this inside her, bottled up, and it spills out onto the page. If you like books to be straightforward, linear, and plainspoken, then you will struggle to get through this one. I have a pretty high tolerance for that kind of thing, though, and I felt like Chime wouldn’t be the same if Briony wasn’t allowed to speak like Briony. You can’t get inside her head and then expect it to be all straight lines and open doors. I thought it was even darkly funny at times. It took me a couple of chapters to settle in and adapt to the rhythm of her words, but you have to remember that it’s not even just the rhythm of her words you’re trying to get used to — it’s also the rhythm of her thoughts.

Beneath the narration, though, is a spirited girl forced to walk the same circles over and over again. It’s no wonder Briony sounds loopy. Who wouldn’t be? Imagine believing yourself responsible for hurting your sister, your only sister. Imagine believing yourself the culprit in your stepmother’s demise. These are heavy weights to bear. And Briony has long since decided to bear them on her own. That is, until the arrival of Eldric, one of my absolute favorite male protagonists to date. Seriously, I am having great luck with the male protagonists this year. Eldric is practically the polar opposite of Briony in a lot of ways. She’s brooding, antisocial, and prone to self-flagellation. He’s sunny, gregarious, and determined to befriend the entire universe. But somewhere between them is common ground. Their chemistry is the kind that just makes you grin idiotically at the page. And it takes a lot for me to admire romance in a book, mind you, so you ought to be really impressed right now. Normally I just roll my eyes.

Briony and Eldric together are just so sweet, in the most non-mushy and wholesome way possible. It’s the kind of romance I like best, because it’s the kind of romance that doesn’t happen overnight. And it’s also the (rare) kind of romance in which neither character loses their sense of self in the process. Briony remains Briony, if grown and a bit changed. Eldric remains Eldric, if also grown and also a bit changed. It’s believable. It made me feel all warm and hopeful inside. I love these two together, the same way I loved Anne and Gilbert, Ella and Prince Char. They become the best of friends. They get into trouble together. They form a secret society comprised of two people who speak awful Latin and practice boxing in the fields. They get angry at one another, misunderstand one another, but ultimately forgive. But if you really want to understand the magic of Briony and Eldric together, I would have to direct you to this snippet of conversation between them at the very end of the book: (WARNING: SORT OF A SPOILER)

I look at the ring. “How did you know it would fit?”

“I don’t know the twelfth declension,” he says, “but I know how you like your cream and jam. I know every one of your fingers.”

That’s love built out of morning after morning of sitting across from one another at the table, eating breakfast and talking about nothing at all. It’s why I want to sleep with this book under my pillow. In the end, Briony learns that it’s not about the past and what horrors might be lying there. It’s about how she deals with it, moves on and grows because of it, and it’s about learning to live in the light. To accept that happiness is real and that she deserves it.

RATING: 5/5

I’m making this sound like it’s solely a love story, but it’s not. It’s a story about coming to terms with who you are, about learning to love and forgive yourself. There are also witches, fey spirits straight out of old English folktales, a cardinal red motorcar, secret initiations by the light of the moon, multiple acts of arson, and awful limericks. In short, it contained many things I enjoy, and that’s why I’m rating this so highly. I emphasize that it’s not for everyone, but I also emphasize that it’s a good book regardless. Not every good book fits into conventional forms. Some of the best, and my most favorite, have not. I’m rating this with a perfect score not because it’s without flaws, but because that’s how it made me feel: happy, intrigued, warm and fuzzy, a little creeped out. I like books that take up residence in my heart and demand to be read again. I’ll be coming back to this one, for sure.
