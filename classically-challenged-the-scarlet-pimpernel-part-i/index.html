Classically Challenged: The Scarlet Pimpernel, Part I

December 6th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Classic | Classically Challenged | Features

Previous Recaps: Persuasion by Jane Austen (Parts 1, 2, 3, 4)

The Scarlet Pimpernel by Baroness Emmuska Orczy

Okay, so apparently you people think it’s entertaining when I try to read classics and then butcher them write ridiculous recaps that I post on our blog. Fine! Now this is happening every month! I’m not reading Austen this month though… instead, you’re getting recaps of The Scarlet Pimpernel every Friday, complete with a running list of all the amazing antiquated swear words I’ve picked up while reading. Zounds!

In a Nutshell: Basically, this is a story of a charming time in history (1792) when the guillotine was the spectator sport of choice in France. Rich, aristocratic people are being decapitated left and right, and not all of them deserve it. Their only champions are the dashing British gentlemen who call themselves The Scarlet Pimpernel. They undertake daring rescue missions for the sake of Justice. (CAPITAL J OKAY???)

Places You Should Care About:

  • Paris, France – In 1792, the Guillotine Capital of the World. Famous for Marie Antoinette, the subsequent beheading of Marie Antoinette, and having your cake + eating it too.
  • The Fisherman’s Rest – An inn located across the channel from Murderland France in merry England, specifically Dover. It’s owned and operated by Mr. Jellyband, and yes you are allowed to giggle at his name every time it comes up. I do.

People You Should Probably Know:

**NOTE: As of this post, I haven’t finished the book yet. There may be more notable characters later.**

Lord Antony Dewhurst – Fond of daredevilry. His father is the Duke of Exeter. Often called ‘Lord Tony’ for short.

Sir Andrew Ffoulkes – Stalwart companion of Lord Tony; always saying adorable things to the ladies. Stop it, you.

Sir Percy Blakeney – Incredibly wealthy British guy who is considered to be rather dull in comparison to his gorgeous, charming, fashionable wife. Except I think he says the most ridiculously hilarious things ever, personally.

Lady Marguerite Blakeney – Sir Percy’s sassy French wife. Once considered to be “the cleverest woman in Europe.” I’m lazy so I frequently refer to her as ‘Lady B’. Pay attention.

Suzanne de Tournay – Rescued from the murderous wilds of France by the Scarlet Pimpernel and co. along with her mother and brother.

The Comtesse de Tournay – Mother of Suzanne and the Vicomte. Rescued by the SP (see how I’m already abbreviating this?? LAZY) from inevitable decapitation in France.

Vicomte de Tournay – 19-year-old brother of Suzanne. Likes duels. Reminds me of a barking puppy.

The Comte de Tournay de Basserive – The de Tournay family patriarch, left behind in their treacherous homeland and in grave danger of being beheaded soon. Well, crap.

Mr. Jellyband – Jolly innkeeper of The Fisherman’s Rest and staunch ally of the Scarlet Pimpernel. Makes Sally do all the work.

Sally – Mr. Jellyband’s daughter and obligatory buxom barmaid. Forever being told to do stuff.

Armand St. Just – Brother of Lady B and her favorite person in the whole world.

Chauvelin – Shady, shifty-eyed Frenchman who is avidly hunting the notorious Scarlet Pimpernel. An acquaintance of Lady B.

CHAPTER ONE: PARIS 1792

The sun is setting over Paris, and a massive crowd has gathered at the West Gate to see if anyone will be caught trying to escape France. No one wants to be executed by guillotine, but boy is it ever exciting to watch someone else being executed by guillotine! (Did you bring popcorn??) Sergeant Bibot is the laudable figure in charge of guarding the West Gate. Catching fleeing aristocrats is pretty much his favorite hobby. He’s on high alert this evening because a group of royalists calling themselves the Scarlet Pimpernel have been helping aristocrats escape through daring means. Those meddling kids! Their leader is said to be a man, “whose pluck and audacity were almost fabulous.” (THIS SENTENCE IS THE BEST EVER.) These perpetrators leave little scraps of paper marked with a red flower — their namesake — every time they strike a blow against the new French Republic.

Sergeant Bibot is well aware that failure to stop these people could earn him a date with the guillotine, so he’s being super vigilant. After all, another sergeant was just executed along with his entire family for failing to stop the Scarlet Pimpernel. This poor, unfortunate soul, Grospierre, let a cart through his gate carrying a load of empty casks. The Captain of the Guard came rushing over, demanding that they inspect the casks for possible stowaways, but none were found. Later it was discovered that the guards were actually the Scarlet Pimpernel in disguise~! Dun dun dun!

Anyway, Sergeant Bibot is pacing around at his gate, determined to put an end to the SP and their roguish ways. He wanders past a lovely group of women who like to hang out within prime view of the guillotine whilst knitting and getting spattered with blood. Eew? Even more eew: one such charming lady collects hair from the beheaded nobles as souvenirs. She’s all set to depart (along with her gross hair souvenir) when she mentions to Bibot that her grandson most likely has smallpox, no big deal, TTYL! Bibot is horrified and banishes her from Paris. She complies, cackling and brandishing her collection of human hair. But once she’s gone, the real Captain of the Guard arrives on the scene and informs Bibot that the crazy old lady was probably… you guessed it… the notorious Scarlet Pimpernel! MORBLEU!

Fascinating Antiquated Swear Word Alert: “morbleu” – a French interjection similar to “zounds!” and “ye gods!” You’re taking notes on these, right?

CHAPTER TWO: THE FISHERMAN’S REST

We are now across the English Channel at a cozy inn called The Fisherman’s Rest, owned by jolly innkeeper Mr. Jellyband (LOL) and his daughter, Sally the Buxom Barmaid. We also meet some of their regulars, who are having a grand old time in the coffee room alternating between hitting on Sally and making Sally go fetch them more drinks. Some complaining is being done (mostly by Mr. Hempseed) over a recent torrential downpour that’s killing all the delicious English apples and pears, DAMNIT. Also some ranting on oranges and those damn foreign fruits. Really, how dare they? And btw, what’s with those crazy French people who keep murdering each other?? Et al etc.

Speaking of French people, Jellyband is expecting guests from over in the murdering yonder. Lord Tony and his best bro, Sir Andrew, are bringing them over very soon. But then an argument erupts between Jellyband and one of the guests (we’ll call him Shady Guest #1 for now), who seems a bit suspicious really, now that you take a good look at him. He suggests that Jellyband might be swayed by some of those dastardly murdering Frenchmen someday, and how does he know he wouldn’t become a sympathizer after encountering someone persuasive?? Jellyband’s infallible logic: the murdering Frenchmen can’t even speak English, so obviously he’d spot them right away as murdering Frenchmen and know NOT to listen to them and their LIES. Duuuhhhh. Honestly, what a preposterous idea. Sit your ass down and finish that drink, Shady Guest #1. *eye-rollage*

CHAPTER THREE: THE REFUGEES

It seems that the English are generally appalled and enraged at the current situation in France. And yet, no one in the British government seems inclined to interfere with all the murder and mayhem. They don’t think England is ready for another long, drawn out, inconvenient war. Ah, well.

Anyway, Sally comes running in to tell her father that Lord Tony’s horse is in the yard. Lord Antony Dewhurst comes in moments later, followed soon enough by Sir Andrew Ffoulkes and three French refugees: the Comtesse de Tournay and her two children. Sir Andrew seems to have taken quite a shine to the Comtesse’s daughter, the dainty, girlish, and perpetually blushing Suzanne. “So this is England,” she says, being all starry-eyed and obviously thrilled with being in a country that isn’t full of murdering murderers. “A bit of it,” replies Sir Andrew, smiling like the roguish British daredevil that he is, “but all of it, at your service.” OH REALLY??? Smooth, Sir A. Very smooth. I think I like you. (Clearly, so does Suzanne. Get a grip, girl.)

Fascinating Antiquated Swear Words Alert: “Zooks!” and “Odd’s life!” Because you want to swear like it’s the 1700′s, right??

CHAPTER FOUR: THE LEAGUE OF THE SCARLET PIMPERNEL

Everyone is having dinner at the Fisherman’s Rest with these new arrivals when Shady Guest #1 decides to leave, much to everyone’s relief. Meanwhile, no one notices Shady Guest #2 engaging in that time-tested, age-old eavesdropping tactic of hiding under the nearest bench. SNORT. (This is truly espionage at its most sophisticated.)

Toasts are made. But then the Comtesse has a breakdown over the soup, lamenting the plight of her husband (the Comte de Tournay) who is still in France and probably running away from the executioner as we speak. Lord Tony and Sir Andrew feel very badly over this, but they were raised to hide their feelings like true British gentlemen and end up looking “immeasurably sheepish” instead. Awww, come here and let me hug you, seriously.

Inevitably, the talk turns to the League of the Scarlet Pimpernel and their courageous leader, who is also a total ~*mystery*~ and whose true identity has always been cleverly concealed. When asked why they risk their lives to save French aristocrats from the guillotine, Lord Tony says something along the lines of, “It’s all great fun! Tally-ho!” Um, I love this dude. He goes on to describe it as pulling the hare from the teeth of the hound in a fitting hunting metaphor. After all, the English “love a bit of good sport.” (Is Danger your middle name, Lord Tony??)

The Comtesse says that someone named Marguerite St. Just, a former actress back in France, ended up denouncing the Marquis de St. Cyr — an act that condemned him to execution. She says some uncharitable things about this Marguerite, but then Lord Tony interjects and says that this woman is none other than Lady Blakeney, currently one of the most fashionable and influential women in London. She married a wealthy British guy and is now the talk of the town. No one seems to think she could do something as dastardly as being part of the bloody French revolution.

This is when Jellyband finally decides to tell everyone that Lady Blakeney and her husband, Sir Percy Blakeney, are actually coming to the inn that night as well… #Awkward
