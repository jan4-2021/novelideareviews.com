CHAPTER FIVE: MARGUERITE

In the previous chapter, jolly innkeeper Mr. Jellyband finally decided to tell everyone that more visitors were expected: Sir Percy Blakeney and his wife, Lady Blakeney. (SURPRISE!!!) This turns out to be totally awkward because the conversation at dinner just veered from the Comtesse de Tournay sobbing hysterically over her soup to suddenly talking a whole lot of smack about Lady Blakeney, who was once Marguerite St. Just — a former French actress/socialite whose testimony sent an entire family to the guillotine. While the Comtesse has a pretty low opinion of this Marguerite, most everyone else can’t believe Lady B would ever do such a thing. Suzanne de Tournay even has fond memories of going to school with Marguerite. (And if you already don’t remember who all these people are, backtrack to Part 1! THERE WILL BE A TEST. Not really.)

In the resulting mess of confusion and awkwardness that ensues, the group votes to hide the refugees upstairs while Sir Percy and Marguerite are at the inn. Great plan, except for the part where they totally failed at doing so. The Blakeneys arrive at The Fisherman’s Rest and promptly encounter the de Tournays before they’re even halfway up the stairs. (Insert ‘fashionable oaths’ from Lord Tony here. LOL.) Within moments, the Comtesse has managed to gravely insult Marguerite Blakeney by insinuating that she’s a two-faced snitch and probably an enemy of France. (Well, this is what I gathered from the conversation, anyway.) All who witness this exchange are utterly mortified.

The Comtesse forbids Suzanne from interacting with Marguerite, but she doesn’t listen and gives the lady a big hug anyway. Definitely the Disney Princess thing to do.

CHAPTER SIX:Â  AN EXQUISITE OF ’92

“Money and titles may be hereditary, but brains are not.” Such are the personal beliefs of Lady Marguerite Blakeney, known by all and sundry to be clever, fashionable, French, and extremely wealthy. All of London was astonished when boring, regular British dude Sir Percy brought home such an interesting wife. No one thinks he’s really a match for someone once generally referred to as ‘the cleverest woman in Europe’ but doesn’t appear to particularly care. Nor does he seem to notice that his wife is constantly pelting him with subtle insults and not so subtle sarcasm. Everyone is a fan of Marguerite Blakeney (except the Comtesse, of course), while Sir Percy is just sort of there.

At least, that’s what this marriage looks like from an outsider’s point of view. But more on that later! In the meantime, the Vicomte de Tournay, all of nineteen years old, takes it into his silly head to challenge Sir Percy to a duel. After all, his mother insulted Lady Blakeney AND turned her nose up at her. Odd’s life! By dueling him, Sir Percy could defend his wife’s honor. Unfortunately for the trigger-happy Vicomte (who always looks just like a puppy in my head when I imagine him as I read… LOL), Sir Percy would rather marvel at his challenger’s “demmed marvelous” grasp of the English language than do any dueling tonight. Duels, according to Sir Percy, are “demmed uncomfortable things.” (They make you late for dinner!! Err, sorry, Hobbit interjection…)

Once the dueling plans are suspended, Lady B departs the inn to say goodbye to her brother, Armand St. Just, who is heading back to Murderland France. Meanwhile, I hug Sir Percy because he’s pretty demmed hilarious. La!

Fascinating Antiquated Swear Words Alert: Zooks! Odd’s fish! Odd’s life! Now use all three in one sentence!! Note: they can also be referred to as ‘fashionable oaths’ because that’s even better.

CHAPTER SEVEN: THE SECRET ORCHARD

Marguerite is saying goodbye to her dear brother, Armand. The two are (almost creepily) close. She’s not all that keen on him returning to their guillotine-obsessed homeland. The two are Republicans, not royalists, but they can’t deny that things have gone just a wee bit too far in France these days. Neither of the siblings agrees with the Decapitate ALL the Nobles! approach to government, which is why Marguerite doesn’t really want Armand to go back there. If anyone discovered that his resolve as a supporter of the New French Republic was wavering, Armand would be in just as much danger as the nobles being marched to the guillotine.

During this chapter, we discover that (GASP!) Marguerite actually loves her husband more than anyone else could ever guess. (I suppose those were affectionate insults??) But she doesn’t think he loves her anymore, not since she told him the truth about her part in the execution of the Marquis de St. Cyr. She confides as much to Armand, who feels that Sir Percy would surely forgive her for her testimony if he understood the circumstances that motivated her to speak against the Marquis.

Privately, Armand muses that it’s possible his sister only fell in love with her husband when he fell out of love for her. #Drama! Armand also wonders if perhaps Sir Percy was too factual to understand why Marguerite testified against the St. Cyrs, and therefore unable to forgive her for it. But it’s time to leave, and the siblings bid one another a sad farewell.

CHAPTER EIGHT: THE ACCREDITED AGENT

So why DID Lady B condemn the Marquis de St. Cyr to be executed by guillotine?? It doesn’t seem like a very nice thing to do. And you know, Marguerite DOES seem to be a pretty decent person. She’s funny, witty, friendly and honestly quite likeable. Something that cruel makes her look uncharacteristically tacky, don’t you think??

Well, my friends, the truth is that our Marguerite was out for revenge. Some years ago, her dear brother was in love with Angele de St. Cyr, daughter of the Marquis. He tried to send her a note, middle school style, in order to confess his love (for another example of this method, see: Persuasion by Jane Austen), but Armand wasn’t nobility… so the Marquis had him beaten almost to death for daring to fall for an aristocrat’s daughter. Total jerk move. Marguerite never forgot what happened to her beloved brother. But duels are demmed uncomfortable things that make you late for dinner (snort), so she decided to avenge Armand by alternate means.

Marguerite found out through the grapevine that the Marquis and his family were appealing to Austria for help against the growing French revolution. Recognizing this as the perfect way to bring him down, she opts to denounce him in public, revealing his plans to the Republic. The Marquis is arrested within twenty four hours and is eventually sent to the guillotine along with his entire family, sentenced with treason against France for his correspondence with Austria.

Once this was done, Marguerite found herself horrified by the consequences of her public denunciation. And when she married Sir Percy, she never expected how deeply he would revile her for what she’d done in the name of revenge. His demeanor towards her turns cold and indifferent. “She had all that the world and a wealthy husband can give to a pretty woman, yet on this beautiful summer’s evening … she felt more lonely than that poor tramp who plodded his way wearily along the cliffs.”

Sadly, she walks back to the Fisherman’s Rest. But she encounters none other than Shady Guest #1 (remember him??), who turns out to be an acquaintance of hers from bygone days: Chauvelin, an agent of the French Republic sent to Britain on a mission to put an end to The Scarlet Pimpernel. Morbleu!

Dull pleasantries are traded for a while. And then, Chauvelin asks Marguerite what she knows about the fabled Scarlet Pimpernel. Would she be willing to render a service to France?? After all, the SP is an enemy of her homeland, and therefore an enemy of men like her favorite (and only) brother. Tch, way to hit below the belt there, dude. Chauvelin is convinced that the leader of the SP is a prominent member of London society, and thus Marguerite would have the best chances of finding him. She’s kind of a big deal, after all. He wants her to be his spy.

But Marguerite refuses. She thinks the SP is an admirable organization of (adorable, roguish, dashing, brave) British gentlemen who fight in the name of Justice by helping people escape the Republic’s brutality, and she tells Chauvelin exactly that. Then she sashays back into the inn like a boss. DO IT GIRL.

Chauvelin remains outside, staring at Marguerite in an exceedingly shifty-eyed manner. Hmmmm…

CHAPTER NINE: THE OUTRAGE

Everyone has gone to bed except for Lord Antony ‘Danger’ Dewhurst and Sir Andrew ‘At Your Service’ Ffoulkes. (Yes, I’ve already assigned them nicknames.) They’re hanging out by the fire, discussing dashing, roguish things and yelling, “Goodnight, Jelly!!!” as the innkeeper makes his evening rounds. You guys, stop it. Seriously. I giggled so much I almost rolled off the couch. Also, we’re all just going to call that guy Jelly now.

The two eventually discuss Sir Andrew’s recent trip from France with the de Tournay family in tow and then move on to some Official Scarlet Pimpernel Business. Sir A reports that he actually saw their leader himself for a few moments while they were in Calais. The man/enigma was dressed as an old hag. (Remember her?? Or, if not her, then her gross hair souvenir??) “His cheek is preposterous, I vow!” Ahahahaha STOP IT LOL.

The Scarlet Pimpernel wants Lord Tony and someone named Hastings to meet him in Calais on the following Wednesday, ostensibly for the purpose of assisting in breaking the Comte de Tournay out of France. And GASP! It appears that Marguerite Blakeney’s brother might secretly be involved…!

Their leader cautions that the French government has sent a shady, shifty-eyed agent named Chauvelin to hunt them down and urges the boys to be very careful about speaking to one another in public, et al etc. He’s sent some written instructions via Sir Andrew, which the two immediately pore over with immense concentration. In fact, they’re SO focused on this task that they don’t notice Shady Guest #2 emerge from his genius hiding place under the bench and come creeping towards them…

A small scrap of paper flutters to the floor. But before either of our heroes can snatch it back up, Shady Guest #2 attacks them!! Well, damnit. Lord Tony and Sir A are incapacitated quickly, then searched for classified SP intel before being carried off into the night. The attacker is pleased to find evidence that implicates Armand St. Just, which can be used to secure Marguerite Blakeney’s cooperation against the Scarlet Pimpernel. BOOOOO. BOO, I SAY!!!

CHAPTER TEN: IN THE OPERA BOX

We are at the opera in Covent Garden for a performance of Gluck’s Orpheus. Plenty of fancy, important people are around. In Lord Grenville’s box is the dastardly Chauvelin, looking all shady and shifty-eyed, as per usual.

Also in the audience are the de Tournays. Comtesse is being all emo again despite the efforts of her friend, Lady Portarles, who keeps saying, “Odd’s fish!” and making me lol. Grenville himself appears; they talk about France and how it really, really sucks right now. Also discussed: the unfortunate Comte de Tournay, who is supposed to be freed by the Scarlet Pimpernel very soon. This is hopeful news, but the ladies are unhappy over the presence of Chauvelin. Grenville isn’t all that thrilled to be tasked with entertaining the guy for the evening. As a government official, Lord Grenville (England’s Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs) has to be cordial for the sake of diplomacy, since England still needs to be civil with the new French government. Even though they really, really suck right now.

The Comtesse once again expresses her extreme dislike of Lady Marguerite Blakeney, that two-faced snitch! Lady Portarles gives her an earful over this, remarking that Lady B is not an enemy anyone would want to have. Speaking of Lady B, Chauvelin is back in his box being super creepy and staring at her in a disturbing manner. Just… eew.

Lady B is in another box, enjoying the opera and ignoring everyone else. Of course, that’s when Chauvelin sneaks in and tells her that Armand is in grave danger. He relates the story of how he found incriminating papers on Sir A and Lord Tony after rudely ambushing them at The Fisherman’s Rest. He still wants Lady B to help him catch their leader. In an act of totally obvious blackmail, Chauvelin hints that Armand could be executed for his involvement with the SP. He shows Marguerite the scrap of paper that fell out of Sir Andrew’s wallet that night. It says, “I will be at Lord G’s ball.” Guess who else is going to be at the ball, in prime position to spy for Chauvelin??

Marguerite is trying really hard not to play into Chauvelin’s hands, but now her brother’s life is at stake. She feels that she has no choice. Briefly she considers asking her husband, Sir Percy, to help her out of this little bind that she’s in… but she changes her mind. Chauvelin looks creepily pleased when she agrees to spy on Lord Tony and Sir Andrew at the ball that night. Eeeewwwwww. And also, nooooo Marguerite!!!

*punches Chauvelin in the face*

*utters a number of fashionable oaths*
