CHAPTER ELEVEN: LORD GRENVILLE’S BALL

We leave the opera for Lord Grenville’s ball, where all the popular kids have convened for the night. Chauvelin stands off to the side, indifferent to the fact that those in government are only being civil to him by obligation. He also doesn’t give a crap that no one really likes him because of the murdering murderers he represents. We are totally not surprised to learn in this chapter that Chauvelin is a huge, huge fan of the guillotine.

Close encounters of the awkward kind ensue when the Prince of Wales arrives, arm in arm with none other than Sir Percy Blakeney and his wife, Lady Marguerite Blakeney. The ballsy Marguerite dares to wear red flowers as a subtle show of support for the Scarlet Pimpernel and shows no sign of being intimidated by Chauvelin. Lord G introduces the Comtesse de Tournay, leading to a really fun exchange of icy pleasantries between her and Lady B. Cat fight?? (Zounds?????)

More awkwardness when even the Prince of Wales reveals himself to be a massive Scarlet Pimpernel fanboy right in Chauvelin’s shady, shifty-eyed face. Our villain takes it all in stride. After all, the night is young…

CHAPTER TWELVE: THE SCRAP OF PAPER

Marguerite is having the worst time ever at the ball. She hasn’t managed to find a way out of the awful deal she made with Chauvelin, nor can she derive any comfort from her husband; Sir Percy is WAY too busy being completely awesome and composing fantastic poetry about, “that demmed elusive Pimpernel.” (Bless you, Sir Percy. BLESS YOU INDEED.)

From the center of her crowd of admirers, Marguerite sees Sir Andrew and Lord Tony arrive at the ball. She’s over come with the burning need to know who the Scarlet Pimpernel really is, a wish that stems from a deep admiration of the man and all of his heroic deeds. But she can’t stop thinking about her brother Armand and the danger that could befall him if she doesn’t spy for Chauvelin as promised. And so, when she sees Sir Andrew finally pry himself away from the truly engrossing activity of making big, sparkly doe eyes at Suzanne de Tournay, she starts watching his every move like a hawk. A HAWK, PEOPLE.

Marguerite witnesses Lord Hastings passing Sir Andrew a note (middle school style!) and pretends to have a classic swooning fit to trick him into thinking she’s totally harmless and not spying at all. Ah, the myriad tools at a lady’s disposal! Whilst pretending to be a-swoon, Marguerite catches Sir Andrew trying to burn the little scrap of paper from Lord Hastings and snatches it away from him. She reads it quickly, then nearly sets the room on fire in her efforts to make him think she didn’t read a single word. Then, she asks him to dance. Damn, Lady B. Quit working for Chauvelin — you’re too good for him! I mean, his people/wannabe spies think professional espionage means HIDING UNDER A BENCH, for crying out loud…

CHAPTER THIRTEEN: EITHER, OR?

Marguerite Blakeney dances the minuet with Sir Andrew, all the while certain of two things: (1) that little scrap of paper said that the Scarlet Pimpernel himself would be in the supper room at 1:00am and (2) Sir Andrew no longer suspects her at all, thanks to her demmed impressive skills as an actress/spy/minuet dancer/ALL THE THINGS. When the dance is over, she walks off with the Prince of Wales, who declares that his life would be a “dreary desert” without the Blakeneys. (He talks about “dreary deserts” a lot.) Marguerite must decide within the next two hours whether to betray her beloved brother or the dashing Scarlet Pimpernel. *suspenseful music*

CHAPTER FOURTEEN: ONE O’CLOCK PRECISELY!

(Yes, this chapter title came with an exclamation point. Because Baroness Orczy had a fabulous sense of humor. I like her~)

Marguerite performs her role flawlessly throughout supper, while Sir Percy’s Scarlet Pimpernel poetry is heartily sung to the tune of ‘Merry Britons’ and the Prince of Wales lols his face off the entire time. She hopes something will happen to intervene with fate and get her out of this bind she’s in, but no miracles are forthcoming. Shucks.

Creepy old Chauvelin pops up to question Marguerite on her work as a spy. She tells him what she knows, ignoring the rest of us who are all screaming at her to shut up. SHUT UP, MARGUERITE!!! AUGH!!!! Lady B feels terrible, but now Armand will be safe. Chauvelin leaves her for the supper room, intent on learning the Scarlet Pimpernel’s identity when he rolls in at 1am. He thinks/ponders on some shady, shifty-eyed thoughts before realizing that the room isn’t deserted at all — asleep on the couch in the corner is SIR PERCY BLAKENEY.

Believing he can still set a trap for the Scarlet Pimpernel, that rat bastard Chauvelin goes to find his own couch to “nap” on as he lies in wait.

CHAPTER FIFTEEN: DOUBT

Chauvelin pretends to take a nap, while Sir Percy may or may not actually be napping. Who knows? It’s Sir Percy. Meanwhile, Marguerite had sent Lord Fancourt out looking for her husband earlier in order to tell him that she’s tired and wants to go home. Lord Fancourt gets back to her at last and tells her that he found Sir Percy taking a siesta in the supper room… and that Chauvelin was in there too.

As Lady B is leaving the ball, she talks with Chauvelin for a moment with the intent of ensuring that he won’t go after Armand any longer now that she’s done as he asked. He only says that her brother’s fate still hangs by a thread. Awesome.

CHAPTER SIXTEEN: RICHMOND

Warning: I am not at all coherent for the following paragraphs.

Sir Percy and Lady Blakeney go home sometime in the wee hours of the morning. Marguerite is still feeling bitter, restless, and morally compromised, so she lingers in the garden for a while thinking emo thoughts and such. She runs into her husband and tries to get him to stay with her, but he refuses. OMG!! Get therapy guys!! Please??? Because omg the feeeeelssss akshfjksd. And also QUIT PULLING A WENTWORTH, SIR PERCY. SERIOUSLY.

Marguerite entreats her husband to listen to the truth about her part in the St. Cyrs’ conviction and subsequent unfortunate trip to the guillotine not long after. He seems to be looking at it in a different light after her explanation, and she begins to realize that maybe she was wrong about Sir Percy… what if he never stopped loving her, after all?? And THEN Marguerite realizes that all she wants is to win him back. OH MY GOD. JUST BEEEE TOGETHERRRRRR SKJDHFJKSDHF

But Sir Percy points out that when Marguerite told him about the St. Cyr debacle before, she gave him no explanation and expected him to prove his love for her at the expense of his honor. Well, crap. YOU DON’T MEAN IT PERCY!!!!

… At this point, my notes on this chapter devolved into a mess of handwritten capslocking and shameless lack of punctuation. Evidence:

MARGUERITE TELLS PERCY ABOUT THE ARMAND SITUATION. I SHRIEK FOR A WHILE. Sir Percy listens carefully and even promises that he’ll try to save her brother, but then proceeds to devote all his energy into NOT snatching her up in his arms and hugging her to death. WHY PERCY. WHYYYY!!! YOU KNOW YOU WANT TO DAMNIT. Just, seriously. Morbleu, zounds, AND odd’s life. And ALL the antiquated swear words right now. FORREALS. ASJKDHFJSD.

And then of course OH MY GAAAHHHH Marguerite STILL doesn’t tell Percy the rest of the story because WHO THE HELL KNOWS WITH THESE TWO. MARGUERITE WHYYYYY. Can’t you see that Sir P wants to believe you?? Except you totally broke his heart before so now he’s being all Wentworth on us! ASKJDHFJK. I CAN’T DO THIS YOU GUYS. THIS CHAPTER. JKSHKJHDF.

Uggghhhh okay (I HAVE TO GET A GRIP) so Marguerite finally heads up the stairs and into the house, back to thinking that maybe her husband does NOT actually love her anymore after all. But!!! BUT!!! If she’d only looked back, she would’ve seen Sir Percy COMPLETELY FACEPUNCHING ME WITH THE FEELS.

THANKS A LOT PERCY. NOW MY FEELS ARE A TOTAL SHAMBLES. DONE DONE DONE I AM DONE. AUGH. And no, I will NOT tell you what he did, because I want you to read it and join me in being a train wreck.

CHAPTER SEVENTEEN: FAREWELL

Lady Blakeney goes upstairs to brood over Sir Percy. (So do I. Ajkhsdfjhsd.) She is now determined to win back his affections, no matter what she has to do to achieve that. As she’s falling asleep, she hears footsteps outside her room and a letter from her husband shoved under the door. It says that he has to leave immediately due to pressing business up north, among other vague things, and doesn’t have time to say goodbye to her in person. Confused as to what kind of business Sir Percy could possibly have at such short notice, Marguerite goes running after him.

She manages to catch Percy before he rides away on his horse, Sultan. (OF COURSE SIR PERCY’S HORSE IS NAMED SULTAN.) As always, Sir Percy is impeccably dressed and back to acting all cold and distant, et al etc. She begs to know where he’s going, and at last he divulges that he’s off to do as he promised and help save Armand from Chauvelin.

CHAPTER EIGHTEEN: THE MYSTERIOUS DEVICE

Lady B hears from Sir Percy’s groom that he’s off on their little schooner, the Day Dream, for whatever business he’s tending to on such short notice. She has no idea why he would need the Day Dream for this, but has no time to contemplate because Suzanne de Tournay is coming over today. Marguerite may have strategically cornered the Comtesse during Lord Grenville’s ball and invited Suzanne in front of the Prince of Wales, thus making it impossible for the Comtesse to forbid her daughter from visiting. Nice, Marguerite.

Dressed and ready for Suzanne’s imminent arrival, Marguerite wanders past Sir Percy’s super secret lair and is suddenly possessed of the burning desire to go in there. In a show of very low self control, she sneaks in and sees piles and piles of very organized receipts and paperwork that don’t go with her husband’s lackadaisical, foppish personally at all. This confirms for Marguerite that Percy’s outward behavior is only a mask. Well, it’s about time, Margot. Seriously. I love you, but boy are you dense sometimes. I mean, the dude has maps of France and Paris hanging up in his room. CAN YOU STILL NOT SEE IT???

As she hurriedly leaves Percy’s rooms, Marguerite steps on something that’s rolled onto the floor: “a solid gold ring, with a flat shield, on which was engraved a small device.” That device, my friends, IS A SCARLET PIMPERNEL.

DUN DUN DUN
