Classics Retold: Persuasion for the Austen-Challenged, Part 4

October 9th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Classic | Classics Retold Project | Features

About Classics Retold | Our Intro Post

Ack!! Sorry for the delay on this!! I’ve had a crazy week (and a half…?) since the last installment of this recap — first I caught the worst cold I’ve ever had in my life, then I was subsequently slammed with work and endless amounts of Grown-Up Responsibilities that ended up leaving no time at all for blogging. *sobs* Anyway, here is the fourth and final post in my Persuasion recap! If you missed the previous installments, here they are: Part I (Intro & Chapters 1-7), Part II (Chapters 8-12), and Part III (Chapters 1-7).

Classics Retold may be over, but Alix and I have decided that my shenanigans recaps will carry on as a feature on the blog! (This is the part where everyone runs away screaming.) So, at the end of this post I’m giving you a chance to tell me which Austen classic I should recap next! Please remember that with this great power comes great responsibility, et al etc.

DISCLAIMER: This is the grand finale of Persuasion, so brace yourself for ALL THE FEELS!

Chapter Seven

Oh snap! Mrs. Clay, are you now gunning for Cousin Elliott instead of Sir Walter??! You gold-digging minx, you! Not that this worked since Cousin E decided to go walking in the rain with Anne. Which, of course, is when Wentworth finally decides to come back into our story! (A wild Wentworth appears! SNORT) After seeing this other dude taking walks in the rain with Anne, Wentworth decides that he ALSO wants to do this. Plus he wants to show off his shiny new umbrella. But Cousin E interrupts the conversation, and a stroll with Anne is not in the cards for Wentworth.

As the days pass, Wentworth sightings continue to take place all around Bath. Even Lady Russell takes some time to stare at him. She covers this up by pretending to be staring at some really snazzy drawing room curtains instead. Smooth, Lady R. Anne says nothing about this but does make some private commentary on “the elegant stupidity of private parties.” Because she’s Anne, and sometimes snarky, even if she doesn’t ever say these things out loud.

By the end of the chapter, everyone is in full freak-out mode over an upcoming concert. Anne doesn’t really want to go… until she realizes that Wentworth might be there. #Gamechanger

Chapter Eight

Â Wentworth does indeed attend the concert. He and Anne have a brief chat about Benwick and Louisa’s surprise engagement, during which Wentworth expresses his own concerns that the two aren’t alike enough. He can’t believe how easily Benwick got over his fiancee, the tragic Fanny Harville; “A man does not recover from such devotion of the heart to such a woman!” OH REALLY???? HMMMMMMMMMM

Anyway, Wentworth adds that he does feel responsible for what happened to Louisa. Anne makes some sympathetic noises but mostly just shrugs and is like, “Ah, well, but I do hope I get to go back to Lyme someday cause that place was awesome. Just saying.” This is because she’s secretly rendered incoherent by Wentworth’s mini-speech about never being able to get over certain women one falls in love with. Is there hope in this awkward, angsty situation after all??!!! Except before any more of this riveting conversation can continue, Cousin Elliott appears on the scene and proceeds to go through with THE MOST UNWANTED INTERRUPTION OF ALL TIME. SERIOUSLY, DUDE?????

A dismayed Anne watches Wentworth return to his own seat while Cousin Elliott starts confessing that he’s heard all sorts of lovely things about Anne and that he thinks very highly of her AND ALSO BTW I’M GOING TO SORT OF PROPOSE TO YOU NOW IN THE MIDDLE OF THIS CONCERT. (Yes, this happened.) Anne rendered speechless; resolves to flee ASAP. After intermission, she makes up her mind to go sit next to Wentworth and all the readers in the world stand up and just cheer for hours on end UNTIL COUSIN ELLIOTT COMES BACK TO RUIN THE MOMENT AGAIN DAMNIT GO AWAY JSKDHFKJSDHF

SDHFFHJSGDFHJSFKJHDJKFH

Chapter Nine

In the days following the concert, Anne devotes all her energy to avoiding Cousin Elliott like the plague. She decides to visit her old friend Mrs. Smith, her former governess, who (if you remember from last installment) is now a destitute cripple but still a very sweet lady. She figures no one in her family, including Cousin E, would be caught dead consorting with destitute, crippled widows and that Mrs. Smith’s house is the safest place to hide right now. But when she gets there, Mrs. Smith starts being super weird about the whole Cousin E thing, going so far as to tell Anne that she expected to hear news of an engagement that day, and that she thinks Cousin E is a great guy, et al etc. Anne is confused and chooses to investigate further, whereupon Mrs. Smith suddenly pulls a complete 180 and condemns Cousin E as a Grade A Douchebag… what is going on??!!

After a considerable amount of hemming and hawing, Mrs Smith divulges that she has known Cousin E for quite some time. In fact, he used to be a very close friend of hers through her late husband, back when Cousin E was much less monetarily endowed and would come hang out with them/mooch off their food and hospitality all the time. Apparently he married his first wife (now also deceased) for her money rather than marrying Anne’s sister Elizabeth way back when. Mrs Smith produces an incriminating letter that proves what a lying, scheming, gold digging brigand Cousin Elliott really is. Behold, a list of his dastardly crimes:

  • Assisted in ruining the Smith family finances, eventually leading to the demise of Mr. Smith and leaving Mrs. Smith a destitute, crippled widow.
  • Was the executor of Mr. Smith’s will, and yet refuses to assist Mrs. Smith in claiming property and money that is due to her and which might just reverse her fortunes.
  • Decided to re-insinuate himself in the Elliott family’s good graces when he realized that Mrs. Clay, that (fellow) conniving, gold-digging minx, had plans to try and marry Sir Walter. And lady, this town ain’t big enough for the both of us. /cue gunfight in Austen novel
  • Is now planning to foil Mrs. Clay’s marriage prospects AND marry Anne himself…!

After listening to all this, Anne realizes just how close she came to being persuaded YET AGAIN to make the wrong decision. If she’d listened to Lady Russell’s babbling about how awesome it would be to marry Cousin Elliott and become mistress of Kellynch Hall just like her mother used to be, Anne would be in the same capsizing ship all over again. She resolves to tell Lady R everything she’s just learned about the true depths of Cousin E’s utter douchebaggery.

Chapter Ten

Like a true professional Queen Douchebag, Elizabeth manages to “send her love” to Lady Russell while simultaneously putting down her taste in books and ridiculing her fashion sense. Hey, this kind of jackass behavior takes skill, okay? Speaking of jackasses, Sir Walter tacks on HIS love to Lady Russell while also criticizing how disheveled she looks in the morning. Ah, the apple doesn’t fall far from the tree~

With perfect timing, another bad apple rolls into town — none other than our favorite crybaby hypochondriac, Mary! Right behind her are a bunch of the Musgroves visiting from Uppercross. Anne’s visit to Lady R is put off as she is pulled into social calls with the Musgroves and learns all about the upcoming weddings planned for Louisa and Henrietta. She is genuinely happy for both of the girls and mostly enjoys seeing everyone again (minus Mary, cause who would really want to see her??) but of course Wentworth soon arrives to have dinner with them all and brings a whole lot of Awkward with him. Anne and Wentworth are careful to stand on opposite sides of the room while secretly wrestling with inner turmoil. Mary, renowned busybody, happens to glance out the window and see some sort of furtive exchange going on between Mrs Clay and Cousin Elliott out on the street. This gets everyone talking about Anne and Cousin E; Anne is consequently upset that all of Bath seems to think she’s marrying that punk. Eeewwwwww.

After the pleasure of watching Mary and her husband have another of their fun little marital squabbles, Sir William and Elizabeth crash the party in order to invite everyone to THEIR party on the following night. Because, appropriate.

Chapter Eleven

On the following day, Anne is hanging out at the hotel with the visitors from Uppercross. Mrs. Musgrove and Mrs. Croft (wife of Admiral Croft, tenant at the Elliott family’s estate, Kellynch Hall) are avidly discussing short engagements versus long engagements and seem to favor the latter option. Long engagements are apparently the worst idea ever. Wentworth pretends he isn’t listening. Pffftttt WHATEVER, WENTWORTH. Captain Harville (seafaring friend of Wentworth) randomly starts talking to Anne about his late sister Fanny, who was engaged to Benwick. He shows Anne a portrait of Benwick that had been commissioned for Fanny, but which is now being made over for Benwick’s new fiancee Louisa Musgrove. He laments that Fanny would not have “forgotten” Benwick as quickly as Benwick forgot about her.

Anne agrees, and in her BEST speech ever in the whole book, she makes a fantastic argument basically stating that women do not forget love as easily as men do. Wentworth continues to pretend that he isn’t listening…

BUT THEN HE PASSES HER A NOTE (MIDDLE SCHOOL STYLE) AND IT IS THE BEST NOTE EVER AND JUST EEEEEKKKKKKKKKKKK KJDHFKSJDHFKJSDHFKJSDFHKJSDHF THIS IS THE SOUND OF ME DYING OF THE FEELS

(in my notes this moment is marked by me writing the word EEEEEKKKKKKK in gigantic all-caps letters that take up like 5 lines)

***Pause for customary moment of unabashed flailing after being facepunched by feels***

Chapter Twelve

WENTWORTH AND ANNE RENEW THEIR ENGAGEMENT. YESSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSSS

Sir Walter finally writes this down in his precious book of the British Peerage (LOL). Lady Russell is made to writhe in agony as her long-ago prediction of doom and misfortune is proven COMPLETELY WRONG. Ha!!! Mary, of course, decides to take all the credit for her sister’s match with Wentworth but also suffers quite a bit of jealousy over Anne’s shiny new carriage. Cousin Elliott leaves Bath; and, since gold-digging birds of a feather flock together, it seems that Mrs. Clay has switched from chasing Sir Walter to chasing Cousin Elliott. Eeewwww. Despite feeling bad that she’s causing Wentworth to marry into a rather shitty family, Anne lives happily ever after with her beloved Wentworth (and a lifetime case of Wentworth Fever ;D). YAY HAPPY ENDING!!!

~ THE END ~

Okay, people! That’s it for my epic 4-part recap of Jane Austen’s Persuasion! Because it seems like our readers have enjoyed the series, Alix and I have decided to make it a permanent blog feature! This means I will be dissecting a classic of your choice! (Or occasionally my choice… if held up at gunpoint… KIDDING! About being held at gunpoint anyway. Err…)

My first official book for the Classically Challenged feature will be The Scarlet Pimpernel by Baroness Emmuska Orczy, just in time for Diana Peterfreund’s Across a Star-Swept Sea, which is a retelling of the book!

BUT! I am taking votes for my next Jane Austen read, which I may or may not try to read at the same time — I’ll assess my level of Crazy and get back to you on that later. For now, you get to decide which of these books I will be reading next for the new Classically Challenged series here on the blog. The choices were hand-picked by our resident Austenite, the lovely Alix!

Choice #1: Emma by Jane Austen

Beautiful, clever, rich – and single – Emma Woodhouse is perfectly content with her life and sees no need for either love or marriage. Nothing, however, delights her more than interfering in the romantic lives of others. But when she ignores the warnings of her good friend Mr Knightley and attempts to arrange a suitable match for her protegee Harriet Smith, her carefully laid plans soon unravel and have consequences that she never expected.

Choice #2: Sense & Sensibility by Jane Austen

Marianne Dashwood wears her heart on her sleeve, and when she falls in love with the dashing but unsuitable John Willoughby she ignores her sister Elinor’s warning that her impulsive behaviour leaves her open to gossip and innuendo. Meanwhile Elinor, always sensitive to social convention, is struggling to conceal her own romantic disappointment, even from those closest to her. Through their parallel experience of loveâand its threatened lossâthe sisters learn that sense must mix with sensibility if they are to find personal happiness in a society where status and money govern the rules of love.

Choice #3: Mansfield Park by Jane Austen

Taken from the poverty of her parents’ home, Fanny Price is brought up with her rich cousins at Mansfield Park, acutely aware of her humble rank and with only her cousin Edmund as an ally. When Fanny’s uncle is absent in Antigua, Mary Crawford and her brother Henry arrive in the neighbourhood, bringing with them London glamour and a reckless taste for flirtation. As her female cousins vie for Henry’s attention, and even Edmund falls for Mary’s dazzling charms, only Fanny remains doubtful about the Crawfords’ influence and finds herself more isolated than ever.
