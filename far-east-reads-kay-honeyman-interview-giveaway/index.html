In what ways are you and Jade Moon alike? In what ways are you different?

We share a love of stories. I find them just as magical, comforting, and full of promise as Jade Moon does.

We are both stubborn and strong willed. I house my stubbornness at my very center, while Jade Moon broadcasts hers to everyone around her. Mine is more like a metal file that wears people down. Jade Moonâs is like a sledgehammer. Hers works better on the page. Mine probably works better in life.

What is your favorite scene in the book, and why?

I love the scenes when Jade Moon is learning to fight with Neil. Those two are so perfectly paired to drive each other crazy.

I also love the scene when Nushi says good-bye. It breaks my heart every time I read it even though I know she has to leave and Nushi has to let her.

Maybe my favorite scene is when Jade Moon tells the story to the women on Angel Island. It is this moment where Jade Moon is not a Fire Horse girl, but just a girl, trying to carve out a life she can live. It is a moment when she is her best self and she is combining her own boldness with the sympathy she feels for these women. I hope the women and the reader get a glimpse of who Jade Moon could be.

Presently, headstrong and independent women are more accepted in society. If the time period shifted and The Fire Horse Girl was set in the modern day instead of the 20s, what kind of obstacles do you think Jade Moon would face?

I think she would still be challenged by her sense of justice and her stubborn determination. Sometimes I wonder where a Jade Moon would be in todayâs society. Would she be crossing the Mexican border? Confronting cyber bullies at her school? Trying to join the high school wrestling team? There are always boundaries to push and lines that should be crossed. And luckily there are people like Jade Moon to do it.

A lot of her story was built around the questions of identities and labels, starting with the Fire Horse sign. These labels are not about who we are as much as peopleâs reactions to who we are. I just finished Divergent (a wonderful story!) by Veronica Roth, and I can imagine Jade Moon in that society struggling with the same boundaries, expectations, and issues of identity.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Kay Honeyman grew up in Fort Worth, Texas and attended Baylor University, graduating with a Bachelors and Masters in English Language and Literature. The Fire Horse Girl (Arthur A. Levine/Scholastic) is her first novel. She currently teaches middle school and lives in Dallas, Texas. You can visit her online at www.kayhoneyman.com.

Hugs and gratitude to Kay for this incredible interview, and for the story of Jade Moon — a heroine that any girl can look up to, no matter what her ethnic background might be.
