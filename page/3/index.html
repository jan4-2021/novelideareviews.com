Don’t Forget Your Towel: my favourite scifi reads

November 30th, 2013 | Posted by Alix in November Scifi Month - (4 Comments)

Today I’m talking about my top 3 favourite sci-fi reads. Picking these were hard! I wanted to give you guys and idea of the different kinds of sci-fi I’ve read as well as the titles which have had the most impact on me. I hope you enjoy this list and maybe pick up a recommendation or two!

The Handmaid’s Tale by Margaret Atwood

Offred is a Handmaid in the Republic of Gilead. She may leave the home of the Commander and his wife once a day to walk to food markets whose signs are now pictures instead of words because women are no longer allowed to read. She must lie on her back once a month and pray that the Commander makes her pregnant, because in an age of declining births, Offred and the other Handmaids are valued only if their ovaries are viable. Offred can remember the years before, when she lived and made love with her husband, Luke; when she played with and protected her daughter; when she had a job, money of her own, and access to knowledge. But all of that is gone now… (from Goodreads)

I read The Handmaid’s Tale the first time when I was 20 and to say that it shook me to the core would be an understatement. This book help me define myself as a feminist. After reading The Handmaid’s Tale, I started seeking out and reading more about women’s right. This book made me promise to myself that I would not allow our society to become like the one depicted here.Â Not only does about weave this beautifully written but incredibly disturbing world, she also makes her readers feel to the very core the outrage and disgust of this dystopian world. I’ve since read the book Â a couple of times and every single time, its impact is the same.

As a sci-fi,Â The Handmaid’s Tale is set in a dystopian world where class structure rules. We’re never specifically told what lead to society’s downfall but we don’t need to, the horror of the women’s camps and their servitude is horrifying yet when you stop and think about, you can see how society could go down that path. Their future is not so distant and that is part of what makes this novel have such an impact. I Â loved that as a sci-fi read, it addresses women’s rights, something I haven’t encountered in my other reads. I cannot recommend this book enough. Do yourself a favour and pick it up if you haven’t already read it.

Ender’s Game by Orson Scott Card

Yet growing up in an artificial community of young soldiers Ender suffers greatly from isolation, rivalry from his peers, pressure from the adult teachers, and an unsettling fear of the alien invaders. His psychological battles include loneliness, fear that he is becoming like the cruel brother he remembers, and fanning the flames of devotion to his beloved sister.Â Is Ender the general Earth needs? But Ender is not the only result of the genetic experiments. The war with the Buggers has been raging for a hundred years, and the quest for the perfect general has been underway for almost as long. Ender’s two older siblings are every bit as unusual as he is, but in very different ways. Between the three of them lie the abilities to remake a world. If, that is, the world survives. Ender’s Game is one of those classic sci-fi novels you just have to read! I think everyone has probably said everything there is to say about Ender’s GameÂ so I’ll be brief. This book blew my mind! Seriously. I was lent it by my “sci-fi buddy”, he told me if I was really getting into the genre, I had to read it. I was skeptical at first. It sounded like aÂ Harry Potter set in space type read. Boy am I glad I didn’t let that stop me! The story is disturbing and the psychological trauma Ender suffers is written so powerfully that youÂ feel for him. The futuristic world makes you ponder morality, abuse, grey areas and all the good stuff in between that sci-fi is known for.Â Ender’s GameÂ also does an excellent job of doing what a lot of modern day YA tried to do and fails: created a hero who is must act and do things beyond his age in a believable manner. My big pet peeve with YA is that teens, sometimes quite young teens, are doing stuff and acting much older then their years and I don’t by it. Ender is forced to behave like and adult and literally has the fate of the world on his shoulders and yet through out the story, I never lost sight of the fact that he such a young boy. His age plays a role in his actions, which I found made the story work.

For me, this book was my first “dark” sci-fi read. Up until then, I had been reading The Foundation series and had just started The Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy. I was knew to the genre and didn’t know how I would respond to the dark material and well I loved it, and it set the tone for the next sci-fi books I tackled.

Snow Crash is one of those books that simply blew my mind. It had again a harsh view of a dystopian future. This was my first cyber punk sci-fi novel and I instantly feel in love with the sub-genre. I love the counter culture, the snarkyness and yes, the darkness (what can I say, I like my lit to be dark! ;)). Neal Stephenson is a masterful writer. The story starts in one and ended up somewhere I could have never guessed! The way it weaved some things together was fascinating! Snow Crash, unlike the other sci-fi listed here, covers not only one subject but crosses into various fascinating subjects: computer tech, linguistic, religion, symbolism, history, anthropology, and archaeology. Scifi: It’s Not Just A Guy Thing

November 9th, 2013 | Posted by Alix in November Scifi Month - (7 Comments)

It’s a Bird! It’s a Plane! It’s Sci-Fi Month! |Â Scifi: It’s Not Just A Guy Thing

Now you might think this is gonna be another post bashing guys who bash girls who like sci-fi, or about being judge/teased by guys because you’re a girl in sci-fi. And sure, I could write a post about that but this isn’t it. Â One of the things I’ve enjoyed the most in planning out my Sci-Fi Month posts has been reflecting on how I became a Sci-Fi fan. My path started off in television when I was a young teen, swerved into movies Â as an older teen and finally passed through books as an adult. When I was 11 started watching The X-Files (in French no less!), IÂ loved it, I wasÂ obsessed with it! But aside from the 2 friends I would watch it with, I didn’t really talk about how much I loved it. Why? Well because of my mind, only guys liked sci-fi. The biggest obstacle I ever encountered in the sci-fi genre as a fan, has been, well me!

Star Trek was the TV my friend’s dad was obsessed with and it was my friends’ older brothers who were the ones who would “make” us watch Star Wars. If I showed too much interest. I was teased both by guys and girls so absorbed that stereotype and ran with it because it was simpler then to challenge it. I was comfortable in my enjoyment of television sci-fi and the couple of friends who shared Â it (but it was still very hush hush). Â But the wonderful thing about the Internet was that it allowed me to get over it (for TV at least). I met all these amazing women who loved and enjoyed sci-fi as much as me. I never experience guys judging me for it or saying I wasn’t good enough online (which surprised me). We were all fans, all equals and it was great!Â I discovered that there were tons of girls, who just like me, loved shows like The Â X-Files, Sliders, Stargate (just to name a few). I shifted from a completely closeted fan to an out only online fan. I was content. I still refused to watch the “classic” sci-fi because I still saw it as a guy thing. In my circle, it was fine to like “new” sci-fi but the “old” stuff was still labeled a boys club. And books? Well girls certainly didn’t read sci-fi books! Even my girl friends who watch it, said reading sci-fi was “too nerdy” and it was for guys. Again, I just went with it. I didn’t want to be teased.

I stayed like that until about 5 years ago. One of my guy friends was a huge sci-fi, the kind of guy sci-fi fan I had judged and assumed wouldn’t want to share that with me. We were talking once and I mentioned all the sci-fi stuff I liked and he asked me what books from the genre I read. When I said none, Â he was shocked and immediately gave me list of titles. Which I devoured and adored.

Since then I have realized my own prejudices and wrong assumptions deprived me of enjoying and sharing my love of sci-fi with guys. I assumed they wouldn’t want me in their club but all the guys I know who like sci-fi, who I have talked to about it have never done anything but share in my enjoyment of discussing the genre! I was wrong: so to all you awesome, sci-fi loving guys of the world, I am sorry I thought you didn’t want me.

What has been your experience with sci-fi? Do you still think it’s a “guy thing”? Have you ever judge or were judge for liking sci-fi? Let’s talk!
