#4: DEERSKIN

October 18th, 2008 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Retelling | Review - (0 Comments)

TITLE: Deerskin
AUTHOR: Robin McKinley

This novel is another of Robin McKinley’s retellings, but I had never read the fairytale it was based on before picking up the book. Apparently, the original story was about a princess who hid from her abusive father under the skin of a donkey, and was called Donkeyskin rather than Deerskin. Not a very pretty picture. The novel itself contains much darker themes than McKinley has included in her other retellings, sticking much more to the violent side that many fairytales have.

The princess Lissla Lissar is the only daughter of a king and his queen, who is still known as the most beautiful woman in all the seven kingdoms. The story of how her father won her mother’s hand in marriage is well known and celebrated in her country; everyone loves the King and Queen, and the princess lives in their shadows. In fact, they mostly just forget she even exists. The only time anyone notices her, it is to say she bears a striking resemblance to her beautiful mother. The little girl grows up in seclusion, with only a nursemaid, until one day a neighboring kingdom sends a messenger bearing a sighthound to give her as a pet. The puppy becomes her truest friend and companion, and the relationship between them is characteristic of McKinley’s weaving of animals into her novels. The child is given quarters relatively far from the activity in the palace, and eventually finds solace in gardening, as well as training her dog. Suddenly, however, the Queen takes ill and only grows worse by the day. She becomes obsessed with her beauty, which drains away with every passing day of her illness. She commissions a portrait of herself, by the finest artist, and upon her death the portrait is hung by the grieving King in the main ballroom of the palace for all to see. His daughter falls even deeper into the shadows, as he is consumed by the loss of his wife.

Time passes. A ball is held, in which the Princess will be present, and it seems the King has seen his child for the first time. His broken mind sees only his dead wife in her beautiful face. He grows obsessed with her and announces that he will marry his own daughter. The kingdom does not react well to this, and the Princess is horrified. She attempts to dissuade her father, but he is delirious with the grief that never left him, and only orders her locked in her chambers until the wedding day. The night before, he brutally rapes her, leaving her bleeding and broken on the floor. She wakes from unconsciousness to find her dog seriously injured and herself a bloody mess. Somehow she gets up and flees the palace, disappearing into the woods.

I won’t tell the rest, because you should probably just read it, but I will say that I liked the book well enough. I wasn’t as awed and in love with it as I was with McKinley’s other books, but it still has a strong message. I was happy with the author’s choice to include the shadowed side of the story and was also pleased that the fairytale ended up not really being too much of a fairytale; things went wrong, those things were overcome, much pain was experienced, many tears were shed. In the end, everything the Princess gains is well fought for. You end up being immensely proud of her. And you know, I was also pleased that the Prince she meets is not your typical handsome, multi-talented, diplomatic, quintessential prince. Instead, he loves dogs as much as she does, and is kinder than he is handsome.

Definitely a worthwhile read.

RATING: 4/5

Robin McKinley

#3: SPINDLE’S END

October 5th, 2008 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Review - (0 Comments)

TITLE: Spindle’s End
AUTHOR: Robin McKinley

First of all, everyone should know that I am probably Robin McKinley’s biggest fan out there. I love how she writes and I have pretty much loved every book she’s written. Closest to my heart is Rose Daughter, but Spindle’s End comes a close second. I love fairytale retellings, I think fairytales have more to them than what we read in the standard version. Robin McKinley is amazing at retelling fairytales, and this book is no exception. From the very beginning to the triumphant end, it is a living and breathing world where magic is both enduring and unpredictable. The best thing about Robin McKinley is that the worlds she creates need no explanation. She doesn’t even need to introduce them to you. You simply set foot within them, and become a part of them, and before long you no longer question the rules and miracles there; you become an honorary citizen. I can think of very few authors with the same talent.

The fairytale remains beneath the story told here, forming the bare bones and solid foundation for the dimensions that are added. Animals play a significant role, as they usually do in most McKinley novels. Rosie, our secret princess, grows up in a rural area of the kingdom after being spirited there by the fairy Katriona. She has been kidnapped in order to save her life–the evil Pernicia seeks the fulfillment of a curse laid upon her infant head years ago. Rosie is fed and sheltered along the way by the animals of wood and glen, who become her steadfast friends, and whose language she masters. In all, the details are the crowning achievement of this novel. The nuances and small every-day things are what make Rosie’s world your world. And the skillful weaving of each detail with the plot itself makes for a very satisfying read.

RATING: 5/5

Robin McKinley

#2: EAST

October 5th, 2008 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Review - (0 Comments)

TITLE: East
AUTHOR: Edith Pattou

A retelling of the Norwegian fairytale “East of the Sun, West of the Moon,” this book is told through several different perspectives, each with a unique and individual voice. Rose, daughter of a cartographer turned farmer and his highly superstitious wife, has one sibling for each direction on the compass. It is believed that the direction a mother faces when her child is born plays an important part in that child’s personality, and even in his or her destiny. Rose is told she was born facing the gentle and patient East, but her family has been hiding something from her: she is a north-born, her mother’s worst fear. Those born facing north are said to be wanderers and explorers, and Rose grows up in true north tradition. It is as she is growing up that she has several sightings and even encounters with a giant white bear. When misfortune befalls her family, the same white bear suddenly appears at their door, offering them happiness and prosperity in exchange for Rose. Her family objects, but Rose leaves with the bear, who takes her to a hidden castle. Each night of Rose’s imprisonment in the castle, a mysterious figure comes to sleep beside her in the dark of night. It is when curiosity finally overcomes her that Rose discovers the truth: not only is she a north-born, but the white bear who has haunted her steps since childhood is actually a prince bound under a terrible spell. What ensues is Rose’s quest to rescue her bear-prince, which leads her across continents and eventually to the frozen wastes of the north.

The voices that tell this story give it so much more dimension than your standard fairytale retelling. You hear the tale from the heroine’s brother and father, the White Bear, a Troll Queen, and that of Rose herself. Each perspective is distinct, lending its own thoughts and emotions as the adventure winds its course. You hear Rose’s struggle between who she thinks she is and who she knows she is; you experience the slow change in the bear as he begins to retrieve the humanity he lost; you hear the shards of ice crackling dangerously in the Troll Queen’s narration. You wait at home with Neddy for a sister you love and have struggled to protect. You feel the anguish of a father searching for his lost child. The story transcends its fairytale roots and becomes something else entirely: a history. Something possible and real, interwoven with magic and myth. I definitely loved this book!

RATING: 5/5

Edith Pattou

#1: THE SWEET FAR THING

August 5th, 2008 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Review - (0 Comments)

TITLE: The Sweet Far Thing
AUTHOR: Libba Bray

The Sweet Far Thing is the third and final book in Libba Bray’s Gemma Doyle series, which began with A Great & Terrible Beauty and Rebel Angels a few years ago. In Gemma’s final year at Spence, she must bring an end to the events she set in motion, and choose between power and sacrifice.

I picked up this series around the same time I discovered the Twilight series, and while at the time I thought Twilight to be the more effective distraction, it was always pretty evident that this one was superior. Libba Bray is just a much more engaging and spirited writer. Her heroine, the feisty Gemma Doyle, makes Bella Swan look pallid and anemic. Which she probably is. But anyway, I definitely LIKED this series from the start. The writing is excellent, the plot is fairly well-constructed, and the characters are believable. Gemma Doyle’s adventures, beginning in the monsoons and crowded markets of British Colonial India and continuing into the equally seamy world of Victorian London’s opera houses and boarding schools, always becomes more than what you would expect. While dealing with fantasy and the supernatural, namely a cult of women seers who once controlled the boundary between reality and dreams, the series weaves in real problems and dark truths. Libba Bray often writes rather beautifully, but also infuses her characters with humor and distinct personalities. What you would expect is another predictable story of saccharine sweetness. What you get is what Gemma discovers for herself: a blurred line between the world we know and the world we imagine, rendered in full color. Libba Bray does not spare her readers the shadows cast by the ballroom lights, the monster behind the jeweled mask. This book ended the series beautifully. And while I would have liked things to be a little bit more epic at times, it can’t be denied that the ending was fitting and far from forgettable.

RATING: 4/5

Libba Bray
