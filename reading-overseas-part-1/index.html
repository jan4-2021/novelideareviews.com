Reading Overseas: Part I

May 5th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Reading Overseas

Growing up in a military family meant that I traveled frequently. I think that moving every few years has really instilled a certain wanderlust in me; I always want to see new places and learn more about them. This curiosity is what inspired me to explore the concept of reading around the world. Living in the U.S. means that I have easy access to books of all kinds, especially with the advent of e-books and instant delivery of digital titles to iPads and e-readers. And if I want an actual, physical book, that isn’t hard to get my hands on either. But I haven’t always lived in the U.S. — I actually spent several years of my childhood and a year of high school in my native Philippines, where acquiring books was significantly harder. The bookstores didn’t have very much selection and back then, you couldn’t just order a book on Amazon. I occasionally borrowed books at my school library, but public libraries weren’t an option. What’s a bookish girl to do??

These memories spurred me to look beyond my own backyard and find out what acquiring books is like internationally. With the help of a few international book blogger friends, I’m hoping to expand your horizons too! This will be a three-part series of posts. First up: none other than my co-blogger Alix, who also happens to be my absolute favorite Canadian! She’s going first because she actually partially inspired this post when she mentioned once just how hard it was for her to find English books where she lives. Read on to find out what she has to say on the topic!

How do you primarily acquire books? i.e. online, bookstores, library, etc

I mostly buy my books. I used to buy my books in stores at either Chapters or Indigo (Canadian equivalent of Borders or B&N). A few years ago I figured out online shopping for books was cheaper so now that’s how I mostly buy them: either from Amazon.ca or Indigo.ca. I buy all my ebooks exclusively through Kobobooks.com (since I have a Kobo and I’m too lazy to shop around other online retailers). About 2 months ago I finally got a library membership to the national library (we have one national library and then local librairies for eachÂ neighbourhoodÂ in the city) and have started taking out books from there more and more.

What are some challenges you face when trying to get new books or just books you want to read in general?

The main challenge for me is two fold: if I want a buy or if I want borrow a book. If I want to buy a book, often times price is the issue. Books and ebooks are always more expensive in Canada then they are in the US. Sometimes it makes me angry to see an ebook is selling in Canada for up to $5 more then in the US! For the same digital file! In recent years, the price of paper books has gone down and is now closer to US prices but it’s still frustrating to know that you pay more here.Â 

I also find that new releases in hard cover are expensive. I know they are no matter where you live but remember, here it will most likely be an additional $5+! I rarely buy hard covers now unless it’s an author and book I am sure to love. I’m also still surprised at how expensive new ebook releases can be! I have seen new ebooks I’ve wanted that were only $1 or 2 cheaper then the physical book! I mentally still have a block about a digital file being nearly the same price as a physical book.Â 

English isn’t the main language where I live so the English titles sections at local libraries are small and thus have a small collection. The book selection varies highly on the neighbourhood and unfortunately at mine, the selection isÂ meagreÂ as the English speaking community is tiny.Â 

New releases in English also aren’t always available at the library right away when they are released in bookstores. Popular authors and hyped books are usually more readily available. Smaller titles and especially romance and YA titles are also harder to find. This is why I got to the national library now since it’s much bigger and has a lot more English titles to chose from! That being said, I find that they tend to get new releases (that aren’t huge/anticipated titles) some time after the release date (if at all!). I still struggle to find English YA titles in libraires. The section is half a stack at our largest library! It’s a very hit or miss experience for me when I go there to find aÂ specificÂ YA title.Â

Do you encounter delays in publishing or availability of books where youâre located?

Titles are usually published at the same time here as in the US, it can take longer for UK releases to be published here. I’ve been lucky to never have a book that was published in the US not be available here on the same day! That being said, I’ve discovered that some ebook only titles are harder to get here because they get released with small publishers that don’t have a Canadian distribution deal. I’m hoping as ebooks continue to grow in popularity, this becomes less and less of an issue!

Buying English books has always been easy, we’ve have all the English Canadian bookstores here (Chapters, Indigo, Coles – which over the years have all been bought by the same company!). These stores are 99% English books only. As I mentioned before, getting books in English from the library has been the struggle since our libraries aren’t majorly in English!
