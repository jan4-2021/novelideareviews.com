Stormdancer

February 24th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy

TITLE: Stormdancer

AUTHOR: Jay Kristoff

SERIES: The Lotus Wars, Book I

FORMAT: eBook, 337 pages

Several times while reading, I had to put this book down because I was seriously on the verge of tears. True, I have a tendency to over-empathize with fictional characters and their fictional lives, but this is just the effect a powerful story has on me. Yukiko, daughter of the shogun’s hunt master, embarks with her father on a quest most likely doomed to failure: to capture a mythical beast, a thunder tiger, and bring it back alive. This seems even more impossible when you consider that Yukiko lives in a dying world, polluted by the men of the Shima Imperium and their legions of machines. It was once a place where legends stalked through miles of deep forest, where primal forces ruled the skies. But now most creatures great and small have been extinct for decades. What hope is there that they will find a griffin — arashitora — and manage to subdue it? Even simple nightingales are a novelty, now found only in menagerie cages. So when Yukiko finds herself stranded in Shima’s last wilderness with none other than the griffin they’ve been hunting, no one is more stupefied than she is. Stormdancer tells the tale of an extraordinary and unexpected friendship, of love without boundaries. I contend that if it doesn’t move you at some point, you probably have no heart at all. (Yeah, I went there.)

First and foremost, I need to explain why it took me so long to get through the first 100 pages. I won’t go so far as to call them a massive info dump (that connotes writing far less elegant than this), but I will say that a tonnage of information is relayed and you will end up cross-eyed trying to remember and make sense of everything. Kristoff writes in intense, expansive detail. Absolutely nothing is left to the imagination. You will know what everything looks like, smells like, tastes like — the works. This frustrated me because I do like to be given some room to imagine the envision the world for myself. As well, the level of detail is just overwhelming. It bogs the story down, making it sluggish and really pretty tedious in the beginning. Don’t get me wrong — the world building itself is amazing. But when the descriptions are so caught up in minutiae that I’m able to count the gold threads on someone’s sleeve, I have to draw the line. I admit that I was starting to give up on the book because of this. I’m extremely glad I didn’t.

I will also mention that many people will probably need to use the glossary in the back, because there is a lot of book-specific, Japan-inspired jargon going on here. I studied Japanese in school and have had a lot of experiences with that country and its culture, so I was armed with more knowledge than the average reader might be equipped with. I could infer more from context clues, which meant this aspect was hardly a blip on my radar. But let me make this clear: the Shima Imperium is NOT Japan. It is based on Feudal Japan, yes. The culture, the language, the weaponry, these are all borrowed from Japan. But at no point does Kristoff ever claim that Shima IS Japan, so I don’t agree when people point out inaccuracies in word usage or whatever. This is a fantasy set in a place that is reminiscent of Japan, so you can’t exactly nitpick when it is NOT Japan. Because it isn’t! I can accept this easily enough because I do read a lot of fantasy and I don’t read it for accurate depictions of the world I live in. (I already live here, what kind of literary escape is that?) If you can reconcile with the idea that Yukiko’s world is not our own, that it’s separate and doesn’t have to follow our world’s conventions, then you’ll be okay.

Okay, I have that out of the way. You have been warned. I also have to warn you that the bond between Yukiko and the thunder tiger, Buruu, will leave you speechless. It’s a thing of beauty. I can’t even describe it. Suffice it to say that this duo grabbed me by the heart and refused to let go. I never want them to. I will remember Yukiko and Buruu forever this way: wild, covered in demon blood, wings and arms and legs and eyes and hearts entwined. It’s visceral. It’s inspiring. I haven’t been this moved by a fictional friendship since Code Name Verity last year, to be perfectly honest. Browsing through Goodreads reviews, I’ve noticed that even the people who disliked this book still had to admit that this girl and her griffin made an impression. I agree completely. They’re straight up glorious together. Glorious. This is the best word I can come up with, even after recovering from the coma I was in last night after turning the last page. You know, that Amazing-Unforgettable-Book coma. I was just delirious with love and appreciation for this book.

I need to get back to the world of Shima though, because Yukiko and Buruu can’t be experienced without also experiencing their surroundings. A big part of the story concerns the plight of this world, which is on the brink of collapse because of humanity’s innovations. Everything in Shima, which is btw very steampunk-inspired, runs on a fuel called chi that is harvested from a toxic plant called blood lotus. Growing vast acreages of blood lotus, which poisons the soil it grows in, has all but destroyed Shima’s environs. Its people are dying, the animals have fallen prey to extinction, and the increasingly mechanized society is characterized by poverty and suffering on a terrible scale. So what you have to understand is that Stormdancer is not only about Yukiko and Buruu, but about the battle they ultimately choose to fight: the battle for their world, to save it and preserve it.

RATING: 4.5/5

I need to shut up about this book now. I just need you to read it. Immersion is slow to set in, but once it does, you’ll discover that the story has worked its way into your bloodstream. There are samurai in mech suits (I was like.. MINI GUNDAMS?! *CUE NERD TIME*), demons waving bloody femurs, and airships sailing through red skies. There is sacrifice and honor, beauty and vengeance. Pick it up because of Yukiko, tattooed telepathic heroine who is armed, dangerous, and full of heart. Pick it up because of Buruu, ferocious and loyal, who will leave you crying on your couch like a total moron. (I am not embarrassed to admit that this was me last night.) Pick it up because it’s a life-changing experience, the best kind of experience when reading a book.
