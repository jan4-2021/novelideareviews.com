Strands of Bronze & Gold

May 15th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Historical Fiction | Series | YA Fiction

TITLE: Strands of Bronze & Gold

SERIES: Strands of Bronze & Gold, #1

AUTHOR: Jane Nickerson

GENRE: YA Historical Fiction

FORMAT: eBook, 297 Pages

Still mourning the loss of her beloved father, Sophia Petheram is surprised to receive a letter from her godfather — a letter inviting her to travel to his sprawling Mississippi estate and stay with him as his ward. Bordering on destitute now that their father and his income are gone, Sophia and her siblings come to the conclusion that she will accept the invitation and thereby have hope for a better future. She will be taken care of, her every need attended to; in short, she will be gain financial security, something the family has never been able to enjoy. And in the beginning, Sophia does indeed enjoy living at majestic Wyndriven Abbey, lavished with expensive gifts and a new, exceedingly privileged lifestyle. But as time passes, and as the affectionate attentions of her handsome godfather gradually intensify, it becomes clear that Sophia’s beautiful surroundings are nothing but a gilded cage. What terrible secrets lie in wait behind locked doors?

This… was not a pleasant experience for me. To be honest, it was a total train wreck and I have this vague theory that I stayed on board for the entire thing out of grotesque fascination. Either that, or it was the undying optimist in me that kept hoping something would happen to redeem this book before it was too late. Sadly, this was not to be. Strands of Bronze and Gold was extremely problematic for me, from the decidedly useless non-heroine to the disturbing lack of plot. I was drawn to the prospect of a Southern Gothic retelling of Bluebeard, but this one definitely didn’t deliver.

We first meet Sophia Petheram, newly orphaned and the youngest of four siblings, while she’s sitting in a coach and en route to her godfather’s fabulous mansion in Mississippi. She has been corresponding with this man, the very wealthy Bernard de Cressac, since she was only a child. We are treated to many mentions of lavish gifts that were sent to her throughout this lengthy correspondence, as well as a very clear understanding that Sophia adores her godfather despite the fact that she’s never actually met him. Does she adore him because seems to be a good, kind, wonderful individual? Sort of. Mostly it seemed to me like she loved his material wealth and receiving gifts. It bugged me from the start that she comes from a family that never had much money, was the recipient of all these amazing gifts from her godfather, and yet never seemed to show remorse or regret that her siblings got jack shit in comparison. So, from the beginning, Sophia and I were not on good footing.

My distaste for her only intensified. She’s deliberately written as a naive, innocent character and I can understand that. I know why it was done, but I don’t think it was done well at all. Instead of appearing to be merely naive, Sophia came across as utterly vapid. We spend the first quarter of the book listening to her go into rapturous descriptions of her godfather’s opulent mansion, the many shiny new possessions she acquired upon arriving to live with him as his ward, minute details of dresses and camisoles and corset laces, and COMPLETELY missing the fact that aforementioned godfather is creepy as all hell. I get that she’s supposed to be innocent and therefore should be excused from realizing that a man who has her dress up in skimpy foreign costumes for dinner because “it pleases him” is super creepy beyond belief, but I thought she’d get clued in to this faster than she did. Instead, she’s actually enthralled by the weird amount of attention he gives her, and even when she starts to get disillusioned with his possessiveness, she STILL volleys back and forth between, “OMG! I think I’m in love with my godfather!” and “OMG! I think my godfather is a bipolar serial killer!”

… I mean, how do you come to the conclusion that your godfather is possibly a bipolar serial killer but then change your mind and think, oh, maybe I’m just being melodramatic, actually he’s really really nice?? This just boggles the mind, people.

I’m dead serious about this. It takes Sophia about 3/4 of the book to really start to loathe the man, and even then she’s STILL entertaining thoughts of rehabilitating him, maybe influencing him for the better and using her ‘feminine wiles’ (snort) to make Creepy Godfather behave himself. I rolled my eyes so often at her inner monologue that at one point I really thought they’d stay permanently rolled back into my skull. She’s seen all kinds of proof that he’s despicable, crazy, controlling, manipulative… but because she’s your classic Mary Sue with an exaggerated do-gooder personality (excuse me while I gag), she never develops the strength to escape from him, nor even the true desire to do so. Sophia is neither clever nor resourceful. Clues are EVERYWHERE, but she rides right past them on her pony (another gift from Creepy Godfather). It really disgusted me that she went on accepting his gifts and enjoying being spoiled at the price of remaining in this abusive relationship. I know others may see her as a character who starts off naive and becomes stronger and wiser, but I beg to differ. She blunders her way through the plot and somehow, by complete accident, manages to free herself.

The book addresses slavery, but mostly by trying to portray Sophia as the Abolitionist Fairy. She’s not a very effective abolitionist. (If you ask me, she’s not very effective at anything.) She cares about the slaves on de Cressac’s plantation and tries to make friends with them, help them, etc but her dedication to them is lukewarm at best. It was disturbing how quickly she could be distracted from wanting to improve conditions for the slaves — literally all de Cressac had to do was dangle some new jewelry in front of her and she’d go galloping back to him like a small child offered candy. Anyway, I think this review best sums up the problematic portrayal of slaves and slavery in Strands of Bronze and Gold. I’m afraid this will turn into a 10 page essay if I get started on this subject. UGH.

RATING: 1.5/5

I thought this book was burdened by a shallow, essentially useless protagonist. I just can’t bring myself to like or enjoy a novel when I can’t respect the main character. It also didn’t help that a whole lot of nothing occurs during most of the story; all the big reveals pretty much take place within the last 20 pages. I remember being halfway through it, pausing, and wondering why nothing had really happened yet. Then things got really, really bizarre — things like Sophia embroidering tapestries with human hair — and I just. I don’t know. I DON’T KNOW, YOU GUYS. (I think Sophia may have just edged Juliet Moreau out of her spot as my least favorite MC of the year… that, at least, is a feat.)
