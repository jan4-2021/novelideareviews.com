Throne of Glass Novellas

April 14th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Series | YA Fiction - (12 Comments)

TITLES: The Assassin & the Pirate Lord, The Assassin & the Desert, The Assassin & the Underworld, The Assassin & the Empire

SERIES: Throne of Glass, #0.1-0.4

AUTHOR: Sarah J. Maas

GENRE: YA Fantasy

I’ve declared my undying love for Throne of Glass multiple times, so no one should be surprised at just how ridiculously excited I was for this week’s cover reveal of Book 2 in the series. I was pretty much haunting their Facebook page waiting for a glimpse, this is how sad it was getting. I was so, so, SO not disappointed though! The cover is beautiful and I’m beyond thrilled that they chose to match it with the new paperback editions. My only complaint is that I have to wait until August for Crown of Midnight. Yes, this is me crying hysterically. In the meantime, I’ve temporarily satisfied my burning need for more Celaena Sardothien by reading the prequel novellas. I decided to review them all at once, because they’re really much too short to have individual reviews. Not that the length mattered much; each one is its own self-contained plot, and the set is perfect whether you’ve already started the series or are just getting into it.

These four stories fill in the missing details of Celaena’s life before the events of the first book. We meet her at the age of sixteen, Adarlan’s most notorious assassin, and her youth definitely shows. This is Celaena in her glory days — even more vain, headstrong, and reckless than she is when we meet her in Throne of Glass, if you can believe that. But one thing I admire about this author and her heroine is that no one ever makes excuses for Celaena’s flaws. She isn’t perfect, and even with her reputation for being a deadly, efficient killing machine, she has moments of vulnerability and naivete that make her a believable character. Celaena is human, and she makes mistakes. In fact, she makes quite a few mistakes that change her life forever. You can look at these prequels and trace the path of her imminent fall from grace.

Happy Tell-A-Fairytale Day!

February 26th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Features - (1 Comments)

Did you know that February 26 is National Tell A Fairytale Day? Here are some of my favorite fairytale retellings and why I love them!

Ella Enchanted by Gail Carson Levine

A retelling of Cinderella, this was published in 1997 and I was 11 years old the first time I ever read it, but I’m 25 now and I still love it. It puts a new spin on a familiar story by answering the question of why Cinderella was always so obedient. The truth? She had to be. Thanks to the worst gift ever given by a fairy godmother, Ella of Frell has been cursed with natural obedience since the moment she was born. If commanded to do something, she has to comply or feel the pain of the curse’s negative side effects. It doesn’t matter if she’s been told to wash the dishes or jump off a cliff — she has to do it. But does this make Ella into a subservient young lady? Not at all. She’s funny, she’s witty, and she’s extremely strong willed. I love Ella, but I also love Prince Char. They fall in love and slide down stair rails together. It’s lovely, it’s fun, and it never gets old.

Rose Daughter by Robin McKinley

Robin McKinley will always be one of my favorite authors of all time. This is her second retelling of Beauty and the Beast (the first being Beauty), and it’s my favorite of the two. It’s one of my “comfort books” that I go to when I need to cozyÂ  up with something tried and true. In this version of the beloved fairytale, Beauty is the youngest of three sisters who are forced to rebuild their lives in a tiny hamlet miles and miles from the glittering and gorgeous life they had in the city. They’re doing well until their father returns from a trip to the city with a stolen rose and a command from a mysterious beast, in whose castle he trespassed during a storm. The bones of the story are there, but in this one the rose motif takes center stage. Beauty is an avid gardener, and the Beast can’t make his magnificent rose garden grow. They learn to understand one another as Beauty works to bring that garden back to life. Beautifully written, with McKinley’s signature touch of magic.

Spindle’s End by Robin McKinley

I told you, I love Robin McKinley. This is a retelling of Sleeping Beauty in which the sleeping beauty has a gift for speaking to animals, apprentices to a blacksmith, and wants nothing to do with her abundance of golden curls. I enjoy the fact that this book questions whether or not being blessed with golden curls, ruby red lips, teeth like pearls, and a natural proclivity for any and all types of dancing are really blessings at all. No one ever even stops to think about that. Katriona, who ends up spiriting the cursed baby princess away from her botched name-day celebrations, very sensibly wonders why none of the child’s 21 fairy godmothers ever thought to give her gifts like being impervious to mud and rain. This, and many other little details, add new dimensions to the story of Sleeping Beauty.

Entwined by Heather Dixon

Princess Azalea is the eldest of twelve sisters who just lost their mother. Plunged into deep mourning in their drafty old castle, with a kingly father who uses an excess of work to deal with his grief, the sisters find themselves forbidden from the one thing they love, and the one thing that still connects them to their mother: dancing. An unexpected solution comes in the form of a secret passageway that leads to a magical place, where the trees have branches of silver and gold, and where a mysterious stranger invites them to dance their sorrows away night after night. But there’s something wrong with the enchanted ballroom and its host — something that endangers everything Azalea loves. This version of The Twelve Dancing Princesses turns the idea of royalty on its head. The royal family is probably further beyond the poverty line than its subjects, subsiding on an allowance from their kingdom’s parliament. Azalea and her sisters each have distinct personalities, and the book has moments both magical and eerie. A great new look at a well-known fairytale.
