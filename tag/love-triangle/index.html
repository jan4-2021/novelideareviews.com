Born of Illusion

August 2nd, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Historical Fiction | Paranormal | Review - (8 Comments)

TITLE: Born of Illusion (Born of Illusion, #1)

AUTHOR: Teri Brown

GENRE: YA Historical Fiction/Paranormal

PUBLISHED: 2013, Balzer + Bray; Hardcover, 373 pages

Anna Van Housen yearns for stability — for something more than just the illusion of a stable life. After spending most of her childhood living a nomadic existence, she’s privately thrilled that her mother has secured a potentially permanent gig for them in New York. Marguerite, a self-proclaimed medium, will be performing stage shows and seances to earn their keep, with Anna assisting. But even her mother’s incredible showmanship pales in comparison to Anna’s natural gifts, which begin to haunt her with disturbing visions and premonitions almost as soon as they arrive in the city. Danger seems to wait at every turn, and it’s up to Anna to decipher which threats are real and which are only illusion.

So, this was a case where the synopsis ended up being more exciting than the actual plot. I enjoyed the 1920′s setting (very reminiscent of Libba Bray’s The Diviners, although perhaps less detailed) and couldn’t help being intrigued by Anna’s unorthodox job as an illusionist, but never felt like I got the suspense I was promised. While still a fairly enjoyable read, Born of Illusion fell short of my expectations.

The Darkest Minds

July 23rd, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Review | Science Fiction | YA Fiction - (12 Comments)

TITLE: The Darkest Minds (The Darkest Minds, #1)

AUTHOR: Alexandra Bracken

GENRE: YA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2012, Disney Hyperion; Hardcover, 488 pages

A large portion of the events in The Darkest Minds take place on the road, on the run. It’s a dangerous chase through an America where most of the nation’s children have either died from a mysterious epidemic or emerged alive, but forever changed. Ruby, one of the survivors, has been living in an internment camp called Thurmond since she was ten years old. By the time she finds an unexpected escape from Thurmond, Ruby is seventeen and has no knowledge of how to control the strange abilities that landed her there in the first place. This is her story, but a big reason why it works is that it’s also the parallel story of friendship so strong that nothing can shatter it. When Ruby finds three other “freaks” on the road in their beat up black minivan, it changes the course of her entire life. It’s a lesson in trust, loyalty, and survival. I really feel that the bonds between Ruby, Liam, Chubs and Zu provide the heart of this story. Ruby finds a family, and the reader does too.

In order to articulate my feelings about this book, I have to begin at the end. Simply put, the final moments of The Darkest Minds left my heart in shreds on the floor. Ridiculous! And it’s even more ridiculous when you consider the fact that I’d already seen it coming — I’d had this theory percolating in my brain from the time I was about 25% into the story, I knew it was going to happen (if not in this book then in one of the later installments), and yet when it DID happen it still had the effect of a massive facepunch to the feels. Seriously, my concluding notes are essentially nothing more than a long string of expletives. It’s kind of pathetic. But this is the kind of reaction I end up having when a book pulls me all the way under, to the point that I’m emotionally invested enough to care that deeply about its characters and their situation. It’s a good thing.

TITLE: This Burns My Heart

AUTHOR: Samuel Park

GENRE: Historical Fiction; Literary Fiction (Adult)

PUBLISHED: 2011, Simon & Schuster; Paperback, 336 pages

I’m sure everyone has come to a place in life where the paths diverge and there’s no turning back. Often, the biggest decisions we make are the ones we can’t change our mind about once they’re made — or at least, not without considerable grief, not to mention the consequences. Soo-ja, young and impulsive, headstrong and independent, longs for the kind of life her parents resolutely forbid her from living. She has dreams of becoming something more than what they want for her, and when she meets a boy named Min who tells her he can make those dreams come true, she decides to marry him despite her misgivings. Soo-ja is poised on the brink of running full tilt down that path until another man gives her a reason to look back, and to wonder if she made the right choice.

“What if?” This is the question that haunts every page of Soo-ja’s story, which remains one of the most beautiful and heartbreaking I’ve ever read. It’s a book that lingers long after you put it down. I’ve since re-read it twice and recently realized that I’d never reviewed it here on the blog — a shame, considering how much I love it. Since we’re in the middle of our Love Triangles 101 event right now, it seemed like the perfect time to talk about this book; no, it’s not YA, but I think those of us who read a lot of YA will find the handling of love triangles in This Burns My Heart to be much more realistic. Being torn between two men involves more than just having to choose Mr. Right. It also means making a choice between one life over another, giving up one world of possibilities that you might never have again. (And hey, if you’re a big Asian drama fan like me, this book would be the most sophisticated, heart-wrenching K-drama in the universe.)
