Born of Illusion

August 2nd, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Historical Fiction | Paranormal | Review - (8 Comments)

TITLE: Born of Illusion (Born of Illusion, #1)

AUTHOR: Teri Brown

GENRE: YA Historical Fiction/Paranormal

PUBLISHED: 2013, Balzer + Bray; Hardcover, 373 pages

Anna Van Housen yearns for stability — for something more than just the illusion of a stable life. After spending most of her childhood living a nomadic existence, she’s privately thrilled that her mother has secured a potentially permanent gig for them in New York. Marguerite, a self-proclaimed medium, will be performing stage shows and seances to earn their keep, with Anna assisting. But even her mother’s incredible showmanship pales in comparison to Anna’s natural gifts, which begin to haunt her with disturbing visions and premonitions almost as soon as they arrive in the city. Danger seems to wait at every turn, and it’s up to Anna to decipher which threats are real and which are only illusion.

So, this was a case where the synopsis ended up being more exciting than the actual plot. I enjoyed the 1920′s setting (very reminiscent of Libba Bray’s The Diviners, although perhaps less detailed) and couldn’t help being intrigued by Anna’s unorthodox job as an illusionist, but never felt like I got the suspense I was promised. While still a fairly enjoyable read, Born of Illusion fell short of my expectations.

Midwinterblood

May 1st, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Horror | Science Fiction | YA Fiction - (6 Comments)

TITLE: Midwinterblood

AUTHOR: Marcus Sedgwick

GENRE: YA Horror / Science Fiction

FORMAT: Hardcover, 272 Pages

Seven haunting tales intertwine through the centuries in this collection of short vignettes, all coming together to tell one story: the story of fear that never sleeps, horror that doesn’t fade, and love that transcends time itself. Bound together by a common thread, a journalist, an archaeologist, a fighter pilot, a painter, a ghost, a vampire, and a viking are all linked to the startling truth hidden on the remote Scandinavian island of Blessed. Blood has been spilled in the fertile fields; deep secrets swim in the icy sea. With every story, told along with the changing phases of the moon and the slow march of time, we come closer to unearthing answers to questions best left buried.

Err, so I read this book in broad daylight and it still sent chills down my spine. I HAVE NO REGRETS. It was an amazing experience, like watching time itself slip backwards, uncovering connections and revelations with every vignette. At first glance, it seems that seven such disparate characters couldn’t possibly have anything to do with one another. That’s where the magic is: they are connected, all of them, and if you’re watching carefully, the big picture unfolds, coming full circle. Midwinterblood is a tale to be peeled back in layers. The past and the future are superimposed, meeting for a moment and then flying apart. It was a puzzle that I felt compelled to solve.

Spellcaster

April 29th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Contemporary | Fantasy | YA Fiction - (2 Comments)

TITLE: Spellcaster

SERIES: Spellcaster, #1

AUTHOR: Claudia Gray

GENRE: YA Fantasy/Contemporary

FORMAT: Hardcover, 370 Pages

Nadia can feel that something just isn’t right about her new town. It’s a dark, terrible magic that lingers over Captive’s Sound like an unending storm. Descended from a line of witches, Nadia tries to figure out where this darkness is coming from and why it’s there in the first place. This leads her to a boy named Mateo, who in turn has inherited a gift of his own: the ability to see the future in his dreams. And that future is even darker than the sinister enchantment eating away at Captive’s Sound. Bound together by magic and prepared to fight what fate holds in store for them, Nadia and Mateo work to unravel a web of secrets that can be traced back over the centuries to one desperate choice and the descent into madness that followed.

This was an underwhelming read for me. From the synopsis, I guess I was expecting something more. At the very least, it seemed like there wouldn’t be an aggravating love triangle to deal with. And no, there isn’t a love triangle in this book, but then again there’s not much of anything else going on either. Everything that could potentially have been compelling always seemed to fall short, and neither the characters nor their relationships with one another ever felt fully realized. Despite some interesting ideas and lovely imagery, I walked away from Spellcaster feeling disappointed.
