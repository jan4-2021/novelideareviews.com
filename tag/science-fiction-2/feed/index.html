AUTHOR: K. Makansi

GENRE: YA/NA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2013, Layla Dog Press

FORMAT: Paperback, 281 pages

The Famine may be over, but the Resistance has just begun.

When Remy Alexander is fifteen, her older sister is murdered in a cold-blooded classroom massacre. When the Okarian Sector hides the truth behind the attack, Remy’s family goes underground to join the clandestine and growing Resistance movement. Now, three years later, Remy is beginning to find clues about why her sister was murdered. But at the same time, Valerian Orlean, the boy Remy once loved, is put in charge of hunting and destroying the Resistance. As Vale fights to protect his people and Remy seeks justice for her sister, the two are set on a collision course that could bring everyone together – or tear everything apart. (From Goodreads)

The Sowing takes place in a post-apocalyptic world ravaged by environmental collapse and a series of devastating wars that has caused a severe population decline. There is safety and prosperity to be found in the Okarian Sector, but these things come at a terrible price: freedom. Almost everything is dictated, beginning with specialized diets tailored to every individual’s needs and station. Everything is ruled by science and theÂ  Okarian Agricultural Consortium, which is responsible for the breakthroughs in artificial agriculture that ensure its citizens are fed and provided with the best medicine. Not everyone is happy with the way the Consortium handles things; a growing rebellion alleges that what appears to be nurturing is actually manipulative and oppressive.

I have to start by praising the intricate world building in this novel… it’s so detailed and well constructed. You can tell that it was a labor of love for the three authors credited with this story — that’s right, “K. Makansi” is a pen name for three authors, a mother-daughter team that came together to craft the trilogy. I loved that the world building was so in depth, even though a lot of the scientific lingo was honestly over my head. (I’m totally more of an artsy, right-brained type so I’ve never been very good with science… err…) The bad thing about this overabundance of technical detail: I had no idea what was really going on with that most of the time and kind of glossed over those parts. The good thing about it: you can tell that these ladies really know what they’re talking about. The Okarian Sector’s obsession with the science of food is obviously well researched. It’s brilliant concept that also serves to be rather frightening as more and more is discovered about the level of control the Consortium has been exercising over the populace.

The narrative switches back and forth between Remy, now living outside the Okarian Sector among the rebel forces, and Vale, the Okarian Sector’s brightest star. Upon graduating from the Academy with the highest honors, Vale embarked on a mission to bring down the Resistance effort. This makes for some awkward times, since there is a history between the two characters that dates back to the days when Remy and her family were still citizens of the Sector themselves. As far as the narration goes, I liked Remy and Vale equally. To Remy’s credit, despite the brewing romantic subplotÂ  going on (and the looming love triangle…), she was pretty focused on the more important task of making sure the rebellion didn’t tank and burst into flames. I always really dislike it when romance takes precedence over fundamental, legit concerns i.e. surviving in a desecrated world, being part of an outgunned, outnumbered resistance faction, and essentially trying not to get killed every 2 seconds. You know, those things. So props to you, Remy.

I liked both main characters’ voices and the first half of the book had terrific pacing. I think it did kind of lag towards the end, though. My main concern with the writing style was that it did feel rather like an info dump at times, with Remy and Vale both handing out heavy doses of world building early in the story. I felt like I was being told what was happening more than I was experiencing what was happening alongside them. Granted, there is A LOT to cover since, like I said, this is a very intricate setting and there’s so much to learn about it. Still, I was impressed with the writing for the most part and it’s certainly a readable narrative. I loved the banter between the characters, especially Remy’s group of fellow rebels. As a geek, I also liked all the technology, even though I couldn’t tell you how on earth any of it worked. Whatever, it was still awesome stuff. /disappears into realm of nerdiness

Another thing I loved: the diversity in the book’s cast of characters. Definitely refreshing!

RATING: 3.5/5

I think one of the most compelling things about The Sowing is how closely this bleak future could resemble our own. There’s a lot of talk about genetic enhancements, modifications, and the like nowadays. The concepts brought forward in this book could very well be in the cards for us someday. It’s well worth pondering the possible outcomes. Fans of science fiction will be happy with this one, but it will be even more of a treat for anyone as fond of political intrigue as I am. (It’s got tons of it! Conspiracies galore! *happy dance*)

AUTHOR: Michelle Gagnon

GENRE: YA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2012, Harper Collins

FORMAT: Paperback, 336 pages

When Noa Torson wakes up lying on a table with stitches in her chest and an IV in her arm, it’s even more terrifying to realize that she has no memory of how she got there. She narrowly escapes the strange warehouse with her life, but this is only the beginning of a conspiracy that Noa must unravel at the risk of becoming a victim herself. What follows is a desperate race to uncover the truth. Noa will need every skill she has in order to survive and find out what really happened to her, as well as the help of fellow hacker Peter Gregory, the only other person who seems to be on her side.

This was extremely easy to get into and a quick, suspenseful read for me. There’s never a dull moment. Noa is on the run, struggling to stay one step ahead of her pursuers while trying to find answers at the same time. I really liked the pacing; it kept me turning the pages, burning with the need to find answers myself. I mean, I know Noa had a personal investment in this because she was the one they kidnapped and subsequently cut open, but I wanted to know why too because 1) creepy and 2) REALLY creepy. Not to mention the strange side effects she starts to experience… You better believe I had some crazy theories brewing in my head while reading. Uhh, you DEFINITELY don’t want to hear any of them either. I am not exaggerating the crazy. LOL.

I liked that Noa was a character who’d been through a lot, from a childhood spent being utterly failed by the foster care system to recent years living off the grid on her own. She’s got street smarts and an incredible will to survive. I loved that she was such a quick thinker, because I’m a huge fan of resourceful heroines who work with what they’ve got to accomplish whatever it is they’re trying to do. She was often on the losing end of the spectrum here, with such a huge enemy to evade, but Noa did everything she could think of to stay one step ahead. But as tough as she is, perhaps the best thing about her is that she is also a very vulnerable person inside. There are quiet moments between chases and epic escapes when this side of her is revealed. She’s a believable character, I think. In a situation like this, it would be insane to not be afraid or be at a loss sometimes. She does waver, and she does get scared. What matters is that she never lets it stop her.

As disconnected as Noa has been from others her own age, and from people in general, you can expect a certain amount of social awkwardness to happen. She does have a hard time relying on others, especially Peter, who comes from this entirely different background and couldn’t even begin to imagine all the things she’s been through at such a young age. He grew up with the certainty of a roof over his head and assurance that his next meal wouldn’t need to be scavenged for. Throughout the story, Peter marvels at Noa’s resilience all the time. He’s just genuinely floored by how she handles things and readily admits that he’s a long way from being anywhere near on par. But I didn’t think of Peter as a weak character… actually, I felt that he was a pretty complicated person and I think also very well-realized. He doesn’t fall into all the stereotypes he could be categorized under. He always comes through in a pinch. And you know, I liked that he was a geek.

Of course, I liked all the assorted geekery in this book. It’s great for fellow nerds out there. At the Dark Days tour, the author talked about her experience writing it and how she actually consulted a real hacker to make sure that all the computer lingo and hacking action in Don’t Turn Around was authentic. Peter and Noa are both pretty accomplished hackers. I was worried this aspect would be super difficult to believe, but in the end I think it was handled well.

I have to express all over again how awesome it was to meet author Michelle Gagnon — like I said, I heard her speak at the Dark Days panel when it was here in Vegas, but I didn’t grab a copy of Don’t Turn Around until I ran into her again at Vegas Valley Book Festival this year. While I was listening to her chat about her books and the process of writing them, I decided that seeing her twice in one year had to be fate or something. Not that I wasn’t already interested in reading, but this pretty much made up my mind that I was going to get a copy immediately, ask her to sign it, and finally find out why Noa woke up with an incision in her chest and no idea how or why. The mystery!!! I HAD TO KNOW. That, and Michelle is one of the nicest people in the universe. She even remembered me from the Dark Days tour even though I didn’t get a chance to talk to her then. *sobs* Read this for more on that experience + a picture of me with the author. I am NOT freaking out in the picture and you should be proud. Ahem.

I did end up feeling a bit put off by Peter’s immediate interest in Noa. I could’ve done without it. I mean, I’m really glad that nothing actually happens between them in this book because it would’ve been really out of character for Noa to suddenly be okay with trusting someone in that way. But I’m afraid there’s a love triangle waiting for me in the second book and I’m already covering my eyes and screaming, hahaha. Also, when the truth about what happened to her was revealed, I think maybe I was a little bit deflated because I was expecting something more. (LIKE ALL MY RIDICULOUS IDEAS MAYBE??) Of course, it’s only the first book in the series, and I am definitely sticking around to find out what else happens.

RATING: 3.888/5

Yes, okay, I’m giving this book one of my bizarre 3.888 ratings. WHATEVER. GET OVER IT. This is the most accurate way for me to express how I feel. LOL. I do recommend Don’t Turn Around if you’re looking for a suspenseful read with lots of computer geekery (not gonna lie, I totally understood all the World of Warcraft references… err…) and characters you can believe in. I actually already have book two, Don’t Look Now, and I’m excited to find out what happens next! Except I am maybe a wee bit sad that none of my crazy theories worked out in the end… hahahaha

]]> http://novelideareviews.com/dont-turn-around/feed/ 8 Of Beast and Beauty http://novelideareviews.com/of-beast-and-beauty/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=of-beast-and-beauty http://novelideareviews.com/of-beast-and-beauty/#comments Mon, 16 Sep 2013 15:00:00 +0000 http://novelideareviews.com/?p=3300 Read more →]]> TITLE: Of Beast and Beauty

AUTHOR: Stacey Jay

GENRE: Fantasy

PUBLISHED: 2013, Delacorte Press

FORMAT: eBook, 256 pages

The domed city of Yuan is the only safe haven for miles in the harsh deserts of Isra’s dying planet. The lands outside the dome are prowled by the Monstrous — mutated beasts like Gem. They were once human, and are now shunned from the cities where those without mutations dwell in privilege and even luxury. Their safety comes at a terrible price, however; a price that Isra knows too well. The blind princess of Yuan keeps many secrets, not all of which are her own. Her path collides with Gem’s when the starving Monstrous attempt to steal the source of Yuan’s prosperity and fail. What unfolds is a friendship, and then a love, that could change the fate of their world.

I’m a huge fan of fairytales and an even bigger fan of fairytale retellings, especially creative ones. I’ve read quite a few of these over the years, but I have to say that Of Beast and Beauty is by far the most unique version of Beauty and the Beast that I’ve ever encountered. And you know, sometimes I just get this gut feeling about a book… it’s hard to explain, but some books I just KNOW I’m going to love. I read the synopsis and DONE, let me have it RIGHT NOW. This was one of those inexplicable moments of book serendipity for me. (I mean, just read the synopsis… OF COURSE THIS WAS MEANT FOR ME. Hello???)

In addition to being the most unique Beauty and the Beast retelling I’ve ever come across, Of Beast and Beauty also holds the record for creepiest use of that fairytale’s rose motif. Seriously. I challenge you to find creepier roses than the ones in this book. It was only one of the many creative twists the author implemented. In fact, the world building and storyline are so well developed that the original fairytale is more like the bone structure underneath, giving the plot its shape but not constricting it. I’ve found over time that this is my favorite kind of retelling, because you can still trace it back to its fairytale roots, but it can also stand alone as something entirely unique.

I thought Isra in particular was a fantastic piece of character development all around. With every revelation, every little piece of her world that turns out to be a lie, she loses her footing but rallies and goes two steps further than she did before. This girl’s a fighter. Despite being born within the Dome and not having to deal with the unforgiving environs of the desert outside, despite never wanting for food or shelter, Isra is still at quite a disadvantage in the beginning of the story. For one thing, she’s been blind since childhood, which makes the lies that imprison her all the more difficult to detect. But as the story progresses, and as she forms a bond with Gem and begins to question everything she’s been forced to accept, Isra just gets tougher and tougher. She’s determined to deal with things in her own way, with this inherent bossiness that perfectly suits her, but also a deep compassion and empathy.

Usually it’s the Beast that holds Beauty captive, but in this story it’s the other way around. When Gem and his fellow Monstrous are caught trying to steal the roses that are the heart of Yuan’s magic, Isra orders her guards to spare Gem’s life, and he becomes her prisoner. Their relationship starts with them at odds with one another, basically hating each other’s guts, and I loved that things were so complicated. I also loved that Isra was not necessarily in a position of power just because she was a princess; there are a lot of political machinations at work in this story that thrilled me because I enjoy that kind of thing. She was as much of an outcast in the domed city as Gem, if for different reasons. They’re both complex characters with secrets and agendas of their own, with a lot of differences to reconcile between them. This made their romance so much more gratifying to me. It was hard-won, and therefore felt more real and worthwhile.

RATING: 4.5/5

I was so impressed with the depth and creativity of this alternate version of Beauty and the Beast. If you’re a lover of fairytales, high fantasy, or even science fiction (this one has a bit of a sci fi twist to it here and there, although the fantasy aspect is more prevalent), then you should definitely give Of Beast and Beauty a try. This is Beauty and the Beast like you’ve never seen before. I absolutely loved it!

AUTHOR: Alexandra Bracken

GENRE: YA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2013, Disney Hyperion – eNovella, 85 pages

PRECEDED BY: The Darkest Minds

America has been ravaged by an epidemic that robbed the population of its children and transformed the ones who managed to survive. This, combined with an economic crash, has left Gabe with very few options in life. His best chance of securing a future for himself is to become a skip tracer, hunting escaped children and teenagers who have broken out of the so-called reform camps. If he collects the bounties on their heads, he might just be able to save up enough to escape his troubled past. But when Gabe captures a young girl who can’t speak, he discovers that it’s even harder to just walk away. What if the government is wrong, and these children aren’t soulless or inhuman? What if giving this girl back to her captors would be the biggest mistake of Gabe’s life?

I absolutely loved this novella! I’m a huge fan of The Darkest Minds (as some of you might have been able to surmise by now, considering how often I babble on and on about it) and I really feel like this short story is the perfect bridge between the first and second installments. Fans of Zu, rejoice! Masochistic readers who somehow enjoy being Facepunched By Feels, also rejoice! This one is a must-read if The Darkest Minds broke your heart into teeny tiny pieces, then tossed those pieces into a raging bonfire. Just… be ready for the pain. In fact, start bracing yourself now. RIGHT NOW.

So I always really liked Zu in the first book, and running into her again in this novella was a lot like bumping into an old friend. And by bumping into an old friend, I mean finding her on the edge of a wilderness, pursued by ruthless bounty hunters. What I love about Zu here is that she’s a total scene stealer. We get to know her in a way that we weren’t able to in book one, because that centered mainly on Ruby. I really appreciated the added depth to Zu’s character — you don’t realize just how mature she is for her age until you meet her again in this story, and honestly I never noticed just how damn sassy the girl is! Even though Gabe is the one narrating, once Zu comes into the picture you really can’t help but be drawn to her.

I ended up loving Gabe too, despite the fact that he comes off as a total douchebag in the beginning. There’s so much desperation in him, so much pain and bitterness, and I felt like he embodied all the displaced, hopeless people who were forced to live around the Kid-Killer epidemic and the failing economy. Desperation leads people to do terrible things, but it doesn’t necessarily mean that they’re bad people. A huge theme in the series so far is survival, and there are many different ways to fight for that. Gabe’s method is to push his feelings and reservations aside in order to become a skip tracer, because he doesn’t know what else to do. I loved the transformation that took place when Gabe met Zu. I loved that Zu seemed to find this goodness inside Gabe that even he couldn’t see.

… AND THEN MY HEART ENDED UP IN SHREDS ON THE FLOOR AGAIN. Who didn’t see THAT coming??

RATING: 4/5

My warning to you: the e-novella may SAY that it’s 85 pages long, and it is, but that’s because it includes the first chapter of Never Fade, which is out in October. So don’t end up like me and be totally, completely facepunched by the last chapter, which I didn’t think even WAS the last chapter. It seriously was like having a door slammed shut in my face. I WAS NOT READY FOR THAT AT ALL. Ugh. I have nothing else to say. I don’t want to spoil it for you. In fact, I think I’ll just go curl up in a corner now and continue the slow recovery process. *sniff* If you haven’t read The Darkest Minds yet, GO DO IT NOW. ]]> http://novelideareviews.com/in-time/feed/ 6 The Darkest Minds http://novelideareviews.com/the-darkest-minds/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=the-darkest-minds http://novelideareviews.com/the-darkest-minds/#comments Tue, 23 Jul 2013 15:00:00 +0000 http://novelideareviews.com/?p=2815 Read more →]]> TITLE: The Darkest Minds (The Darkest Minds, #1)

AUTHOR: Alexandra Bracken

GENRE: YA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2012, Disney Hyperion; Hardcover, 488 pages

A large portion of the events in The Darkest Minds take place on the road, on the run. It’s a dangerous chase through an America where most of the nation’s children have either died from a mysterious epidemic or emerged alive, but forever changed. Ruby, one of the survivors, has been living in an internment camp called Thurmond since she was ten years old. By the time she finds an unexpected escape from Thurmond, Ruby is seventeen and has no knowledge of how to control the strange abilities that landed her there in the first place. This is her story, but a big reason why it works is that it’s also the parallel story of friendship so strong that nothing can shatter it. When Ruby finds three other “freaks” on the road in their beat up black minivan, it changes the course of her entire life. It’s a lesson in trust, loyalty, and survival. I really feel that the bonds between Ruby, Liam, Chubs and Zu provide the heart of this story. Ruby finds a family, and the reader does too.

In order to articulate my feelings about this book, I have to begin at the end. Simply put, the final moments of The Darkest Minds left my heart in shreds on the floor. Ridiculous! And it’s even more ridiculous when you consider the fact that I’d already seen it coming — I’d had this theory percolating in my brain from the time I was about 25% into the story, I knew it was going to happen (if not in this book then in one of the later installments), and yet when it DID happen it still had the effect of a massive facepunch to the feels. Seriously, my concluding notes are essentially nothing more than a long string of expletives. It’s kind of pathetic. But this is the kind of reaction I end up having when a book pulls me all the way under, to the point that I’m emotionally invested enough to care that deeply about its characters and their situation. It’s a good thing.

I wasn’t sure how I felt about Ruby at first. Honestly, I don’t think Ruby knew what to think of herself either. She would have these moments when I just wanted to shake her — get a move on already!! — but even though Ruby is constantly at odds with her abilities, what made me love her in the end was the fact that she never hesitated to do what was necessary to survive. She doesn’t like to use her powers (I won’t tell you about them AT ALL at risk of ruining your experience, but I LOVE them and I was seriously impressed with what Ruby could do), but when it counts, when it’s down to the wire, Ruby comes through every single time. Even if it will be at great cost to herself, she delivers. And I feel like she was one of those characters I’ve encountered who had so much genuine growth by the time I turned the last page. I was pretty damn proud of this girl. Well, and also choking back sobs because of that ending.

And Liam! Omg help?? How is this man so adorable?? Many books have that character whose good heart gets them into trouble every time. Their compassion often becomes their downfall, which is always terrible to see because who likes to watch the nice guy finish dead last? I feel that Liam is that guy, but he’s also more. He’s a risk-taker, a born leader, but there’s this vulnerability to him that Ruby finds herself determined to protect. I loved that Liam taught Ruby how to trust, but even more than that I love Liam for being the light that brought Ruby out of the shadows. Liam gives her a reason to be strong. And that’s when Ruby truly comes into her own, fitting into her skin at last.

America in the aftermath of the devastating “kid-killer” disease is a bleak, practically lawless place. The remaining fraction of the population lives in various levels of poverty, the suburbs abandoned and the highways empty. The author paints a grim portrait of a nation still mourning the loss of its children almost a decade since the first wave of deaths. Most have lost sons and daughters, if not to the epidemic then to the succession of buses that arrived to take the surviving children away. I appreciated the fact that this deadly, often eerie landscape wasn’t just a painted backdrop for teenage melodrama. Our characters are constantly fighting to make it to the next day, dodging skip tracers who hunt them for the bounties on their heads and even having to battle other fugitives in order to secure a safe place to sleep for the night. This journey is taken one step at a time, and practically every move forward is fought for tooth and nail.

RATING: 4.5/5

It’s hard for me to resist a book that elicits this much emotion while I’m reading. I can’t help loving it. I feel like the best books make that connection with you, and The Darkest Minds definitely resonated with me. The only reason I’m taking off points is that the book kind of crawls at times while Ruby and crew are on the road. I feel like it could have been a bit shorter without suffering for it. BUT: I was hooked from the very first chapter, and the characters made me love them, and the ending broke my heart. I’m in this until the bitter end now, guys. I HAVE NO REGRETS. Go read this!!!!

]]> http://novelideareviews.com/the-darkest-minds/feed/ 12 Something Strange & Deadly http://novelideareviews.com/something-strange-deadly/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=something-strange-deadly http://novelideareviews.com/something-strange-deadly/#comments Thu, 18 Jul 2013 19:00:00 +0000 http://novelideareviews.com/?p=2814 Read more →]]> TITLE: Something Strange & Deadly (Something Strange & Deadly, #1)

AUTHOR: Susan Dennard

GENRE: YA Historical/Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2012, HarperTeen; eBook, 248 pages

Eleanor Fitt thought her family’s situation couldn’t get worse. Since her father’s death, money has been increasingly scarce, and now Eleanor’s brother has gone missing, with the only clue to his whereabouts appearing in the form of a letter crushed in the fist of an undead corpse. To make things worse, her mother unwittingly invited a malicious spirit into their home, she’s falling hard for an inventor of ill repute, and corsets make it extremely difficult to outmaneuver the undead horde. Land sakes!

I’ve heard so much about this series that picking it up was inevitable. I confess to knowing pretty much nothing about it when I requested from the library — only that it was mostly well received and had something to do with the undead. Guys, you don’t need very much convincing when it comes to this girl and a good zombie apocalypse. It’s a really interesting concept because there are, in my opinion, so many different ways to handle it. Something Strange & Deadly brings the undead masses to 1876 Philadelphia, and while it was frequently predictable, I still thought it was a fun ride.

It helps that the heroine is easy to get along with. Eleanor Fitt is under a lot of pressure to help undo their formerly affluent family’s fall from grace. All she has to do is make a good match — which is easier said than done, because Eleanor happens to feel very strongly about being an independent woman during a time when girls and women were expected to remain resolutely in the background. It’s a man’s world, and Eleanor is having none of it. She has an insatiable mind, always brimming with curiosity, which of course gets her into twenty different kinds of trouble on a daily basis. It’s always nice to see a heroine who gets herself into scrapes but manages to get herself out of them, whether this means using her head or finding the nearest available baseball bat and taking out zombies with it. I liked Eleanor’s inquisitive nature and her determination to be something more than what society expected of her.

I also thought it was interesting that there were so many mentions of body image. Early on in the story, Eleanor’s late father is quoted as having listed ‘a fondness for buttered toast’ as one of her few vices. A lady is supposed to appear dainty and eat like a bird, plus there’s the problem of fitting into an extremely tight corset, so Eleanor is frequently hungry or even regretting taking an extra bite of breakfast. Her mother criticizes her figure, and Eleanor seems plenty aware that she isn’t the ideal size, but I appreciated that the concept was even included. I feel like it’s overlooked in other books of this genre; yes, the descriptions of beautiful dresses and tiny waists abound, but what about the problems associated with cultivating that image? To me, Eleanor was more down to earth because she struggled with it.

Although the plot was predictable (I was able to guess most of the twists early on, as well as the big reveal), I didn’t feel especially bothered by it. First of all, I was intrigued by the concept of a zombie horde in the middle of 1876 Philadelphia and enjoyed the inclusion of the Centennial Exhibition as a central part of the story. The steampunk & ‘scientific’ paranormal bits were often a bit much and I ended up taking them with a grain of salt, but the author did a great job clearly defining the setting without overwhelming the reader with excessive period detail. It was sort of like… okay, I don’t really see how you could really rig an alarm to go off when the undead are near, BUT that’s fine because the characters are fun and the pacing keeps me from getting bored. (Kind of like those episodes of Ghost Adventures when they hold up some bogus piece of electromagnetic equipment and I just roll my eyes…) There’s a lot of action, with the first chapter opening with an undead attack, and there aren’t any dull moments acting as filler.

By the way, I liked Daniel immensely, but he totally punched me in the face with that ending. I mean, I don’t believe it for a second, but props to the author for choosing that moment in particular to NOT be predictable at all.

RATING: 3.5/5

An incredibly readable book, Something Strange & Deadly is an enjoyable excursion into an alternate Philadelphia where the dead aren’t always truly dead. A sassy heroine and a colorful supporting cast make the predictability more bearable. I can never seem to resist anything that promises time travel. Throw in some star-crossed love and the prospect just gets better! Shandy Lawson’s The Loop brings both of these things and more. Ben and Maggie’s desperate fight to alter their fate is never without the lingering sense of danger waiting just around the corner. Likewise, there’s never a dull moment. The book could easily be envisioned as a film, in my opinion. I found myself intrigued by the concept of ‘loops’ in the flow of time and really became fond of the two protagonists during the time I spent with them. My only complaint here is that the book is so very short — just a little over 200 pages. Those two hundred or so pages are fueled by adrenaline, but expect to be left with some unanswered questions.

This is rare, but I liked both Ben and Maggie equally. Their personalities mesh very well. Fortunately, Ben and Maggie are repeatedly thrust into difficult situations — situations that test how resourceful they can be. They’re forced to face challenges together, but also individually. In the beginning, Ben and Maggie try separating to see if that will do anything to break the loop. I argue that the relationship between Ben and Maggie isn’t anything like the Insta-Love Phenomenon that we’re so often subjected to in many YA books these days. Yes, there’s an instant attraction, but since we begin the book at the beginning of yet another loop in their timeline, it’s really impossible to tell how much of that attraction is really instantaneous. When Ben sees Maggie again, both for the first time and for the hundredth time, he’s drawn to her but doesn’t remember her from the last time they went through this scenario. They’re only able to retain bits and pieces of their repeated experiences, and Maggie is better at retention than Ben. I liked that he developed that affection with Maggie despite not remembering. To me, this meant there was something real between them, an unbroken connection.

It made sense to me that they would feel strongly about one another. Some may complain that they developed those feelings over the course of two days, but I’m pointing out to you that those two days have spooled back on themselves over and over again by the time the story begins for the reader. Just imagine NOT feeling anything — friendship or more — for someone you kept repeatedly getting stuck in a time loop with. Over and over again, Ben and Maggie meet on an escalator in the mall. Over and over again, they spend the next two days trying to escape their inevitable death. Over and over again, they watch each other get killed. There are some strong emotions bound up in something like that.

I thought the concept of being snagged in a time loop was very interesting. You really can’t help hoping that these two will figure out a way to break free of the destiny waiting for them. The problem is complex: they can’t remember everything they’ve tried before, and consequently can’t remember everything that’s resulted. Then there’s the fact that neither fate nor time has any desire to let Ben and Maggie alter their course. They find themselves battling an inexorable current, one that’s determined to push them in the direction they want to avoid. Overall, I thought the plot was well-paced; you can hear the clock ticking, you can feel that desperate rush of fear and determination that characterizes every decision made. I wasn’t confused, and I wasn’t ever bored. Even if the book wasn’t short, I think I would’ve finished it in one sitting. I wasn’t entirely happy with the resolution, though. I wanted something more. RATING: 4/5

I’m rating this high because I enjoyed it. It was short, true. But since I think The Loop‘s brevity does add to its high octane pace, I’m not rating down for brevity. While the science fiction aspect of time travel and loops is intriguing in its own right, I admire Ben and Maggie for putting heart into something that might otherwise have come across as cold and clinical. You know, and they know, that their chances of surviving and breaking the loop are terrifyingly small. They continue to fight, though, and you continue to hope that they’ll succeed, because they win you over. Pick this up if you want a short, suspenseful read with a science fiction twist.
