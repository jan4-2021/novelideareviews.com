Don’t Turn Around

December 3rd, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Review | Science Fiction | Series | Suspense | YA Fiction - (8 Comments)

TITLE: Don’t Turn Around (PERSEFoNE, #1)

AUTHOR: Michelle Gagnon

GENRE: YA Science Fiction

PUBLISHED: 2012, Harper Collins

FORMAT: Paperback, 336 pages

When Noa Torson wakes up lying on a table with stitches in her chest and an IV in her arm, it’s even more terrifying to realize that she has no memory of how she got there. She narrowly escapes the strange warehouse with her life, but this is only the beginning of a conspiracy that Noa must unravel at the risk of becoming a victim herself. What follows is a desperate race to uncover the truth. Noa will need every skill she has in order to survive and find out what really happened to her, as well as the help of fellow hacker Peter Gregory, the only other person who seems to be on her side.

This was extremely easy to get into and a quick, suspenseful read for me. There’s never a dull moment. Noa is on the run, struggling to stay one step ahead of her pursuers while trying to find answers at the same time. I really liked the pacing; it kept me turning the pages, burning with the need to find answers myself. I mean, I know Noa had a personal investment in this because she was the one they kidnapped and subsequently cut open, but I wanted to know why too because 1) creepy and 2) REALLY creepy. Not to mention the strange side effects she starts to experience… You better believe I had some crazy theories brewing in my head while reading. Uhh, you DEFINITELY don’t want to hear any of them either. I am not exaggerating the crazy. LOL.

TITLE: Not a Drop To Drink

AUTHOR: Mindy McGinnis

GENRE: YA Science Fiction/Dystopian

PUBLISHED: September 2013, Katherine Tegen Books

FORMAT: Hardcover, 320 Pages

RELATED POST: Dark Days Tour Recap

In a world where fresh water is rare enough to kill for, Lynn and her mother spend their days on the roof of their house. The focus of every day is defending the pond on their property, which is their main source of water, and which is consequently a prime target for others who are desperate to survive. Life is a battle for Lynn, and it only intensifies when shots in the dark and smoke on the horizon signal both a beginning and an end.

The Boy Who Could See Demons

August 16th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Fiction | Review - (4 Comments)

TITLE: The Boy Who Could See Demons

AUTHOR: Carolyn Jess-Cooke

GENRE: Fiction/Literature (Adult)

RELEASE DATE: August 13, 2013, Delacorte Press

SOURCE: Digital ARC (Netgalley)

As a child psychiatrist, Anya Molokova is familiar with the demons that can plague an unstable mind. She even has firsthand experience, having suffered the death of her own daughter from mental illness. But when she meets a small boy named Alex, she takes on the most baffling, disturbing case of her career. For Alex, demons are real. They congregate in the corners of his room, speak to him in broad daylight, and can manifest at any time. He tells Anya that his best friend is one such demon called Ruen, but he doesn’t tell her that Ruen wants him to do terrible things. Things like hurting other people, and hurting himself. Is Ruen a product of the child’s psychosis, or something else entirely?

I seldom venture into adult lit these days, but I’m always interested in books that deal with child psychology because I work with kids on a daily basis. Of course, there was also the possible occult angle, which made me want to read this one even more. The Boy Who Could See Demons was a relatively short read that covered a number of tough subjects — from the tumultuous history of Northern Ireland to the debilitating effects of mental illness. While at times it had the tendency to sound like an academic paper or someone’s doctoral dissertation, it also unfolded with excellent pacing that kept me guessing until the very end.

Keeping the Moon

July 25th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Contemporary | Review | YA Fiction - (2 Comments)

TITLE: Keeping the Moon

AUTHOR: Sarah Dessen

GENRE: YA Contemporary

PUBLISHED: 1999, Viking Juvenile; Paperback, 228 pages

When Colie dropped her extra pounds, she also dropped a way of life. Everything changed when she and her mother lost weight. Now her mom is known to the entire world as Kiki Sparks, fitness superstar, and she barely recognizes her. Reinvention hasn’t come as easily to Colie; she’s always had trouble making friends, and that was one thing that didn’t change when everything else in her life did. So when she finds herself packed off to spend her summer at the beach with her aunt, she isn’t expecting very much. Certainly not friendship, and definitely not love.

As I’ve said before, being able to predict what will happen doesn’t really bother me when I’m reading Sarah Dessen. In fact, it’s a big reason why I like reading her books. I pick them up because I want something familiar and comforting, and not to have my world turned upside down. I’ve always liked that she’s so good at sneaking something thought provoking into an everyday, ordinary situation. That being said, while I liked Keeping the Moon just fine, I didn’t connect with it as I have with other books by this author. I’d have to say it was the most predictable yet, and for the first time ever, this bugged me.
