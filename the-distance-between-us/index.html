The Distance Between Us

January 15th, 2014 | Posted by Paola in Contemporary | Review | Romance | YA Fiction

Two things: (1) This book was adorable ajdjksfh and (2) Okay, Xander. I am thoroughly charmed. YOU CAN STOP IT NOW. Seriously! I really, really enjoyed The Distance Between Us. As someone who tends to shy away from contemporary, I feel like this is saying something. It’s both undeniably fun and terribly funny. And while the plot twists weren’t what I’d call earth-shattering, it didn’t really matter to me because I loved Caymen and Xander so much. It’s definitely the characters that define this one. And okay, I appreciated all the humor too. I can never resist a book that makes me laugh this hard.

There are a number of reasons why I occasionally swerve into the realm of contemporary. Sometimes I just need a break from heavier reads, or I feel like I need to reconnect with life in general after reading a fantasy book that consumed my entire universe for the past week or so. I also read contemporary when I want something comforting — something I don’t have to bend over backwards to relate to, with situations that will be familiar and conflicts that I recognize because I might have been through them before. I often find them to be pretty predictable plot-wise, and yet for some reason this doesn’t bother me too much in the genre… it’s oddly comforting to know that no curve balls are headed my way. A (good) contemporary book is a nice change of pace, for me. And you know, I’m a hopeless romantic so I enjoy a little sappiness now and then~

The Distance Between Us turned out to be my favorite kind of contemporary read. Ummmm it probably helped that I spent the entire time wanting to hug Xander to death. (XANDER. LET ME LOVE YOU.) But before we get to him, we have to talk about Caymen, because I honestly loved her too. It’s awesome to read about extraordinary characters and the equally extraordinary situations they find themselves in, but it’s also great to read about a regular girl having to deal with things that are decidedly irregular. Between fighting to help her mom make ends meet through their doll shop and keeping up with school on top of that, she’s got a lot on her plate when Xander walks into her life. I completely understood this girl because I know what it’s like to have no choice but to be the responsible one, and I definitely relate to juggling ALL the things. (Anyone who knows me well can probably attest to this — I am never NOT busy!) It’s so easy to get caught up in what you have to be doing and end up bogged down with all these worries that keep you from moving forward.

This, I think, is the main reason why I’m such a huge fan of Caymen and Xander being together. When she’s with him, she can be young and carefree. The worries fall away for a little while, and the burden is a bit lighter because not only does he infuse her life with excitement and unexpected adventures, he’s also someone she can count on. For someone like Caymen whose social circle follows the doctrine of quality over quantity, having another person she can lean on is a pretty big deal. It’s one more person she can trust. It’s hard for Caymen to let go and not be the one solving the problem at hand because she’s just that kind of girl, but Xander helps her learn to be okay with that. He teaches her to lean on other people once in a while, and that there’s nothing wrong with it.

I don’t want to say that Caymen brings out a more responsible side of Xander, because he really isn’t that stereotypical. He was already responsible when they met. They’re both problem solvers, even though the issues they face are on opposite sides of the spectrum. But like I said, Caymen’s problem is learning to let go once in a while, and Xander already has that part figured out. (I mean, it probably helps that he has access to a private jet whenever he feels like it… I would be REALLY good at letting go of my responsibilities if this was an option. LOL.) What draws me to Xander is his effusive, unsinkable, agenda-free optimism. He’s a genuinely nice guy. AND SO ADORABLE. I CANNOT EVEN. I absolutely loved the chemistry between these two, especially the banter exchanged back and forth, which is some of the funniest I’ve ever read. They never miss a beat. He keeps up with Caymen’s razor wit and somehow softens it a bit. Even better, he sees through it and reads the real meaning behind the words she’s saying.

As previously mentioned, the plot twists weren’t anything crazy, though I’d say they were definitely not flat or disappointing either. I did feel a bit conflicted towards the end when it seemed like finding out the truth about her mother was enough to suddenly solve all of Caymen’s problems. If not for how brightly the romance shines in this story, that might have ticked me off a little more. But I can’t help but be forgiving when I feel like the heart of the book lies in Caymen’s process of figuring out that the distance between people can be real or imaginary, but a second glance might reveal that it’s easier to bridge that gap than you think. This wasn’t about a boy waltzing into her life and making everything better, brighter, more beautiful. It was about a girl realizing that she wasn’t alone, after all.

RATING: 4/5

Loved it. Perfect for fans of Sarah Dessen and adorable boys who bring you hot chocolate and walk you to school every morning (YES PLEASE?? Why didn’t I have this in high school?? Oh, that’s right, I had ridiculous melodrama instead…), but also anyone who can appreciate a good joke about creepy dolls. Readers of contemporary — don’t miss it if you still haven’t read it! Non-readers of contemporary — give it a chance, because it might just put a (stupid, silly) smile on your face.

I actually met Kasie West at Vegas Valley Book Festival last year and it was a lovely experience! There’s a picture, along with my very witty and totally incoherent anecdotes, over here at this recap post.
