Throne of Glass Novellas

Published by Paola on April 14, 2013 | 12 Responses

TITLES: The Assassin & the Pirate Lord, The Assassin & the Desert, The Assassin & the Underworld, The Assassin & the Empire

SERIES: Throne of Glass, #0.1-0.4

AUTHOR: Sarah J. Maas

GENRE: YA Fantasy

I’ve declared my undying love for Throne of Glass multiple times, so no one should be surprised at just how ridiculously excited I was for this week’s cover reveal of Book 2 in the series. I was pretty much haunting their Facebook page waiting for a glimpse, this is how sad it was getting. I was so, so, SO not disappointed though! The cover is beautiful and I’m beyond thrilled that they chose to match it with the new paperback editions. My only complaint is that I have to wait until August for Crown of Midnight. Yes, this is me crying hysterically. In the meantime, I’ve temporarily satisfied my burning need for more Celaena Sardothien by reading the prequel novellas. I decided to review them all at once, because they’re really much too short to have individual reviews. Not that the length mattered much; each one is its own self-contained plot, and the set is perfect whether you’ve already started the series or are just getting into it.

These four stories fill in the missing details of Celaena’s life before the events of the first book. We meet her at the age of sixteen, Adarlan’s most notorious assassin, and her youth definitely shows. This is Celaena in her glory days — even more vain, headstrong, and reckless than she is when we meet her in Throne of Glass, if you can believe that. But one thing I admire about this author and her heroine is that no one ever makes excuses for Celaena’s flaws. She isn’t perfect, and even with her reputation for being a deadly, efficient killing machine, she has moments of vulnerability and naivete that make her a believable character. Celaena is human, and she makes mistakes. In fact, she makes quite a few mistakes that change her life forever. You can look at these prequels and trace the path of her imminent fall from grace.

The younger Celaena will be even harder for you to deal with if her personality didn’t mesh with you in the first book. I admit that despite how much I love her, there were many times when I wanted to give her a good decking while reading these novellas. It makes sense though, because being famed for your ability to dispatch grown men with a dagger doesn’t make you older than you really are. She’s seen things no teenager should have to face, but she’s still sixteen, and she still has a lot of growing up to do. These are the hard lessons Celaena had to learn, the lessons that make her what she is. Ultimately, I understand why her faults are at front and center many times throughout the prequels. And again, I appreciate that she has them. It’s those flaws in her character that make her inherent virtues shine through. Yes, she’s incredibly vain, a sucker for superficial and material comforts, and ruthless with other people’s feelings. But Celaena is also compassionate, possessed of a sense of honor and justice that is often the biggest factor in the decisions she makes. As clearly as her faults are outlined, so too are her best qualities.

The Assassin & the Pirate Lord is perhaps the best example of this. When Celaena is sent to retrieve a shipment of slaves for the master of the Assassin’s Guild, she can’t bring herself to follow orders. Instead, she devises a plan to set all 200 slaves free, even knowing the punishment that will await her when she returns. The orders themselves are so fundamentally wrong, so completely opposed to her personal beliefs, that complying with them was never an option. In this prequel we also meet Sam Cortland, the Sam she thinks of often in the first book: Celaena’s rival since childhood, and eventually her first love. It was really hard to allow myself to love Sam because anyone who’s read Throne of Glass knows what his fate will be. I won’t go into more detail to avoid spoilers, but suffice it to say that even though I liked his character very much, I just couldn’t get attached. (Plus, we all know I’m a diehard fan of Chaol…)

My favorite of the four was definitely The Assassin & the Desert, in which Celaena’s punishment for setting the slaves free is three months of sweltering heat and grueling training at an isolated desert fortress. I liked this one best because Celaena is essentially alone, with only her own heart and personal strength to guide her. She’s stripped down to the core of who she is, forced to evaluate her life and the choices she’s made. Behind her bravado is a girl who lost her parents, who was sold as a child to be broken and remade into Adarlan’s Assassin; she’s someone who is supposed to be heartless, but the opposite is true. The duality of her nature is very interesting to me. One of her primary struggles in accepting herself is also accepting that she’s capable of killing, and even capable of enjoying it, but also that she has a choice.

Celaena returns to the Assassin’s Guild and confronts her feelings for Sam in The Assassin & the Underworld. The first of many deep, lasting betrayals takes place in this novella, as her very complicated relationship with her master is explored further. Arobynn Hamel is the man who taught her everything she knows, who gave her a home and a purpose — a father figure, but also a cunning manipulator whose intentions are never fully predictable. Because of her love for Sam and growing disillusionment with life as a hired killer, Celaena makes the decision to leave the Guild for good. But the decision isn’t without consequences, and there is always a price to pay.

The Assassin & The Empire is the final prequel installment and the one where I feel like my patience with Celaena was the most sorely tested. Still, nothing about how she acts is unrealistic or out of character. After coming to the realization that she will never be free of Arobynn’s influence until she puts distance between herself and his schemes, Celaena takes on one last mission. If successful, she and Sam will have enough to leave and start a new life together, far away from their bloody past. We see Celaena struggling to adapt to a lifestyle that is definitely a world apart from her rather luxurious upbringing as Arobynn’s prized pet, and we also see friction in her relationship with Sam. Fiercely independent, undeniably selfish at her very worst, Celaena has a hard time settling into the dynamic of being a couple even though she cares deeply for him. If you aren’t a fan, you will definitely not like her here. Even I wanted to shake her repeatedly. I think that this was necessary though, because without it there wouldn’t be development in her character. And without the terrible lessons she has to learn in the months before her imprisonment in Endovier, I don’t think I would’ve liked her much in Book 1.

OVERALL RATING: 4/5

The prequels are a must-read if, like me, you’re trying to stave off the debilitating symptoms of Crown of Midnight withdrawal. Also if you’re curious about the adventures of Celaena Sardothien back when she was Queen Bitch of the assassin profession, and definitely if you’re wanting to start the series from the very beginning. I love the writing, the pacing, and especially the characterization of Celaena, who is among my favorite heroines of all time and yet is also admittedly one of the most difficult to love. Now that I’ve read these, I do feel like I have a better understanding of Celaena. Actually, I want to go read the first book again… it’s a vicious cycle, people. Anyway, my lovestruck review of Throne of Glass is here, and you should definitely go prepare yourself for the utter badassery that is Crown of Midnight, out this August from Bloomsbury!
