Throne of Glass

March 12th, 2013 | Posted by Paola in Fantasy | Series | YA Fiction

TITLE: Throne of Glass

SERIES: Throne of Glass, #1

AUTHOR: Sarah J. Maas

She was a legend once, her name mentioned only in whispers. Those with power both needed and feared her. In another life, Celaena Sardothien was the realm’s most notorious assassin. But that was before she got caught. Sentenced to one of the king’s famed death camps to pay for her crimes, Celaena hungers for freedom. And the chance to regain it is the last thing she expects to be given when none other than the Crown Prince appears with a proposition she can’t refuse: to battle for the title of King’s Champion, and thereby earn the right to live as she pleases once her years of service to the throne are fulfilled. Celaena is determined to win. Of course, it isn’t that simple. Victory would bring her closer to the freedom she craves, but it would also mean serving a man who has taken everything she holds dear — a tyrant and a murderer, one who would order her to kill regardless of whether she felt it was wrong or right. She was a ruthless killer, once upon a time. But is she still?

Throne of Glass surprised me. The longer I spent reading it, the more I realized that it had practically everything I like to see in high fantasy: a heroine who doesn’t spend all her time dithering about what to do next, all the swords and magic and world-building a girl could ask for, and… GASP! A LOVE TRIANGLE THAT ACTUALLY WORKS?? Read on, brave soul, and I will explain all…

But don’t expect me to be coherent about it. I’m writing this about half an hour after turning the last page and I haven’t fully returned to the real world yet. Bear with me.

Let’s talk about Celaena first, because we all know the main character can make or break a story. If you’re tired of YA heroines who are described with terms such as ‘Plain Jane’ (I especially loathe that one) or as ‘nothing special, really’ and then turn out to be beloved of all anyway, then you’ll be glad to know Celaena is nothing like them. We know from the beginning that she’s special. She was an infamous killer for hire, trained from childhood to excel at her craft. She’s got a bloody past, and when we meet her she’s been toiling for a year in a wretched salt mine. Not that she took her sentence meekly, mind you. It’s part of why I love her. While it seems rather unreal for Celaena to be good at so many things, I was strangely unaffected by it. Normally this would set my teeth on edge, but I think the author handled her heroine very well. She can fight, she secretly loves to read, and she can play a pianoforte like nobody’s business. She’s also beautiful, PLUS she knows it. Yes, we should all hate Celaena… but you can’t. Because she’s also funny, and she has her awkward moments just like all the rest of us. The point is, even though she was a peerless assassin, she was also a young woman — a girl whose childhood was taken from her, a girl who loves a pretty dress and blushes beet red when embarrassed. Celaena is human. It was easy for me to accept that she was a very talented person, because let’s face it: there are very talented people out there. It’s not a crime for someone to be good at a lot of different things. (What I’m trying to say is, if you’re even the tiniest bit insecure… you may not like Celaena very much.)

And on that note, why do people seem to prefer inept heroines anyway? I don’t. I like intelligent, witty, skilled heroines who can hold their own. I like them to know exactly who they are, and never apologize or second guess that. Which, again, is why I liked Celaena so much. She won’t be for everyone, especially if you think fantasy heroines are made more accessible or ‘real’ by how clumsy or awkward they are. Celaena is neither. Not saying she doesn’t fumble, because she does, but she isn’t constantly falling on her face. Sorry if you find that kind of thing endearing. Look for a different book~

Now, let’s talk about why I said the love triangle is actually acceptable in this case, because I’m sure some of you out there are wondering if I may be suffering from a concussion. I make no secret about how much I despise love triangles as a plot device, which is why I was cringing ahead of time when I saw there would be two male characters close to Celaena and in prime position to become Corner 1 and Corner 2 of The Love Triangle. First, of course, there’s Prince Dorian. He’s your standard womanizer with a father complex, though I will grant that he had some hidden depths. But as things go, I wasn’t very impressed with Dorian and really just wanted him to go away. I liked Chaol, our stoic Captain of the Guard, ever so much better. It doesn’t take a genius to realize that Celaena is better with Chaol, although I may be a bit biased in that regard because I LOVE THESE TWO TOGETHER. Dorian was the side show and Chaol the real thing, at least to me, and hopefully to Celaena when the time comes for her to really choose. Because, my friends, there is no obvious choice being made in Book 1. And I thought that was key when it came to making this love triangle work.

You see, subtlety is a beautiful thing. I’m not of the opinion that real love is something that happens instantaneously. In her relationships with Dorian and Chaol, Celaena’s affections are gradual and not sudden. But also, this love triangle works because it isn’t the only thing going on in the entire book. She isn’t defined by any prospective romance. Celaena remains Celaena, with or without love interests — but the beautiful thing is that she is a better, wiser Celaena because of love itself. I won’t tell you which of the two she gravitates toward, but I will say that for the first time in a long time, a love triangle didn’t make me want to bash my head into a wall. I can see her ending up with either. I may not like it if she ends up with one of them over the other, but I appreciate that the author made this believable. And you know what else? I appreciate that, by the end of Book 1, Celaena is done with the process of being torn between the two. Like I said in the previous paragraph, she makes her choice, even if this choice isn’t loudly proclaimed from one end of the kingdom to the other. She chooses with her heart and her head. I approve.

Here’s my one major complaint though — and you’re probably going to think it’s ridiculous. See, even though I think the love triangle worked out and was implemented well, I think it would’ve been fine to not have one at all. Then again, I think this about every book that has one, so feel free to ignore me.

RATING: 4.5/5

Throne of Glass is rife with court intrigue, petty jealousies, and burning ambitions. Almost every character wants something so badly that they will kill — or die– to achieve their own ends. The air is heavy with secrets being kept and secrets being traded as currency.Â The pieces are in play, and the game is just beginning. It’s a dangerous world, and Celaena is dangerous enough to survive in it. As the first in a series, I thought it set up the plot extremely well. There is trouble brewing, and our heroine will be right in the middle of it all when the second installment, Crown of Midnight, is released this August. Needless to say, I’m currently writhing in agony because I have to wait until August to find out what happens next. However, there are four prequel novellas already out there for anyone who wants the lowdown on Celaena Sardothien’s illustrious past.
