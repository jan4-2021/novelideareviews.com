Why I Love Fantasy (Or: Yay! Unicorns!)

Published by Paola on April 16, 2013 | 11 Responses

Day 1 | Day 2 | Day 3

Reading has always been an escape for me. This is probably why I’ve never been a big fan of fiction set in contemporary settings; I just find it harder to become absorbed in a world that is too much like my own already. What I want in a book is to be completely immersed, to feel like I’m decidedly Elsewhere. I want to be caught up in something all-encompassing; I want to walk with heroes, journey through imaginary landscapes. No other genre accomplishes this for me with as much magic as Fantasy, especially High Fantasy, with all its far-off places, daring sword fights, magic spells, and princes in disguise. (Belle and I have had the same taste in books since the 3rd grade.)

Since it’s Epic Quest week here on the blog, I thought I’d write a bit about my history with the genre. We go way back. If you’ve always wondered why I’m so odd (but also awesome) then carry on, brave adventurer.

If we want to get really technical here, my enchantment with the Fantasy genre began when I was four years old. No, it actually didn’t begin with Disney, although I love Disney and don’t give a fig about what others might say. (That’s right, I just said I don’t give a fig. You mad??) This was when my dad brought home a (probably pirated? err) copy of the 80′s animated film The Last Unicorn. *points to image below*

Um, can we say full-fledged obsession?? I not only watched this movie repeatedly, often multiple times a DAY, but also became convinced that I actually was a unicorn. Trapped in a four-year-old’s body. Magical, beautiful, amazing, sparkly — but limited to being under three feet tall and utterly desolate because of that. (Uh-huh, now you know why I’m so messed up.)

“The unicorn lived in a lilac wood, and she lived all alone. She was very old, though she did not know it, and she was no longer the careless color of seafoam, but rather the color of snow falling on a moonlit night. But her eyes were still clear and unwearied, and she still moved like a shadow on the sea.”

To this day, I still watch this movie when I’m sick or need to be comforted. It wasn’t until I was in high school that I first read the book, which btw is infinitely better and more incredibly beautiful than I can even describe. *points to quote above* If you haven’t read it, why not? Every time I open this book, I’m just enthralled all over again. I don’t know how you couldn’t be, with a first line like that. A few years ago my husband gave me a signed copy of the 40th Anniversary Edition as a gift. (…Probably why I’m still married to him. Hahahaha) And last Christmas, I treated myself to a newer, graphic novel edition that is absolutely breathtaking. It never loses its magic for me, even though I know practically every line by heart (and all the songs from the movie version, because I’m lame devoted) This, I think is why fantasy ended up being my first love.

In elementary school, I discovered mythology. It started with Greek mythology, then I moved on to Norse, then I just went all out global. So much of Fantasy is based on myths and legends, fairytales and folklore. I think this only intensified my appreciation and love for the genre. But then I got to this point where the mythology and lore of Earth just wasn’t enough. The next logical step, as you might guess, was Middle Earth. In fifth grade I found an illustrated edition of The Hobbit (specifically this one, illustrated by Michael Hague) and officially kicked off my bad habit of reading by flashlight through the night instead of going to bed like a conventional child.

Ah, the good old days.

Wait, I still do that. Ahem.

Anyway. The genre is far from perfect. It irritates me that so much of classic fantasy, and even newer fantasy, is so guy-centric. The female characters in such books are often relegated to the background, reduced to damsels in distress or as pawns in political games. We have enough of these shenanigans going on in the modern YA scene as it is. I’m glad to say that the genre is evolving, as I hope all books are evolving — to feature strong, independent, amazing women who can hold their own. And I think it’s a testament to fantasy/high fantasy that some of my favorite heroines come from this genre.

I’ve been reading fantasy ever since, and most of my collection of beloved books belongs to that genre. Any time I want, I can pick a far-off land to explore, and that’s just part of the magic. I love that fantasy contains characters that are larger than life, characters that are faced with impossible odds but persevere regardless. I love the grand scale, the emotion, the drama, the romance, the endless possibilities.
